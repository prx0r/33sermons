# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 08

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*

---

## The Teaching

### The Key Verse: Viññāṇaṃ Anidassanaṃ

Towards the end of the last sermon we happened to quote a certain verse from the Kevaḍḍhasutta of the Dīgha Nikāya. The verse runs as follows:

> *Viññāṇaṃ anidassanaṃ, anantaṃ sabbato pabhaṃ, ettha āpo ca paṭhavī, tejo vāyo na gādhati, ettha dīghañca rassañca, aṇuṃ thūlaṃ subhāsubhaṃ, ettha nāmañca rūpañca, asesaṃ uparujjhati, viññāṇassa nirodhena, etth'etaṃ uparujjhati.*
>
> "It is in a consciousness that is anidassanaṃ, anantaṃ, and sabbato pabhaṃ, that earth, water, fire, and air do not find a footing. It is in this consciousness that long and short, fine and coarse, and pleasant and unpleasant, as well as name-and-form, are kept in check. It is by the cessation of consciousness that all these are held in check."

Let us now try to sort out the meaning of the difficult words in the first two lines.

### Anidassana: Non-Manifestative

First of all, in the expression *viññāṇaṃ anidassanaṃ*, there is the term anidassana. The meaning of the word nidassana is fairly well known. It means 'illustration'. Something that 'throws light on' or 'makes clear' is called nidassana. This is the basic sense.

From this we can understand the import of the whole passage. Consciousness (viññāṇa) is so called because it manifests (vijānāti) objects — it knows distinctly. It throws light on name-and-form. An ordinary person sees a name-and-form when he reflects, which he calls 'I' and 'mine'. It is like the reflection that a dog sees when it looks into a stream — it mistakes its own reflection for another dog.

But *anidassana* consciousness is consciousness that does not manifest in this way. It does not throw light on name-and-form as something distinct. It does not take a stand. The word *nidassana* can also be related to the idea of pointing out or showing. When you say "this is it," you are pointing — that is nidassana. Anidassana consciousness does not point to anything as "this." It is non-manifestative, non-indicative.

It is important to note that the commentators tried to interpret this verse as referring to Nibbāna itself rather than to a type of consciousness. But the verse itself says *viññāṇaṃ* — it is about consciousness, about the Arahant's mode of knowing. The attempt to push it into the realm of the unconditioned misses the point: the Arahant's consciousness itself is anidassana, ananta, sabbato pabha.

### Ananta: Boundless

The second term is ananta — boundless. Ordinary consciousness is bounded by name-and-form. It has a limit, a boundary, a "this-ness" that it identifies with. But this consciousness is not bounded by anything. It does not have the limits of "I," "mine," or "self."

### Sabbato Pabha: Luminous on All Sides

The third term is sabbato pabha — luminous on all sides. Not a light that goes in one direction, manifesting one object at a time. But a radiance that is everywhere, without a source.

It is in this consciousness that the four great elements — earth, water, fire, and air — do not find a footing. Long and short, fine and coarse, pleasant and unpleasant — all pairs — are held in check. And name-and-form, together with resistance and perception of form, are fully held in check.

And the last line says: *viññāṇassa nirodhena, etth'etaṃ uparujjhati* — it is by the cessation of consciousness that all these are held in check. This cessation is not the annihilation of consciousness but its "non-arising" — the consciousness that no longer arises to manifest name-and-form.

---

### The Dog Crossing the Stream

In the Khajjanīyasutta, there is a simile of a dog crossing a stream. A dog, while crossing a stream, sees its own reflection in the water. Mistaking it for another dog, it snaps at it, hoping to snatch away the bone it sees in that reflection. But by doing so, it loses the bone it already had in its mouth. So the dog ends up with nothing.

Similarly, the untaught worldling takes name-and-form — his own reflection, as it were — to be his self. He grasps at it. But in grasping, he loses the very thing he thought he had.

The Arahant, on the other hand, does not mistake the reflection for a self. He sees name-and-form as name-and-form — a reflection, empty, not-self. He does not snap at it.

---

### The Mirror Simile

In the Rāhulovādasutta, the Buddha uses the simile of the mirror to stress the importance of reflection in regard to bodily, verbal, and mental action. Just as one looks into a mirror to see one's own face clearly, one should reflect on one's actions before, during, and after doing them.

But the deeper point: what does the mirror show? It shows a reflection. And the reflection is empty. The face in the mirror is not a real face — it's a manifestation of the face. Similarly, name-and-form is a manifestation, not a self. The consciousness that anidassana does not produce such a reflection — or rather, it sees the reflection as reflection, not as real.

---

### The Arahant's Pasture

The next portion of the Kevaḍḍhasutta tells us what the true range or pasture of the Arahants is:

> *Suññato animitto ca, vimokkho yesa gocaro.*
>
> "Emptiness and the signless — these liberations are their range."

The Arahant's pasture is not name-and-form. It is not the world of signs and manifestations. Their pasture is emptiness and the signless — *suññato animitto ca, vimokkho yesa gocaro*. The word *vimokkho* means liberation or deliverance. The liberations themselves — the states of being freed from the tangle — are their range.

The Arahant has gone beyond the need to take a stand (ṭhita) on any object. The worldling's consciousness always takes a stand — it settles on name-and-form, establishes itself there. This is the "established consciousness" we discussed earlier. The Arahant's consciousness does not take a stand. They range in the freedom that comes from seeing through all signs.

The "signless" (animitta) means the mind does not pick out signs from experience to construct a world. It does not seize upon the "this-ness" of things. The "empty" (suññato) means that what appears is seen as empty of self and what belongs to self.

The ordinary person's pasture is the opposite: he ranges in signs, in name-and-form, in the manifestations of consciousness. The Arahant has given up that range for the range of emptiness.

---

### The Cessation of Consciousness

The verse says: *viññāṇassa nirodhena, etth'etaṃ uparujjhati*. The cessation of consciousness is where all these are held in check. But what does this mean?

Consciousness here is the consciousness that manifests name-and-form — the consciousness that arises dependent on name-and-form and gives rise to name-and-form in turn (the two bundles of reeds). When that consciousness ceases — not by being annihilated, but by no longer arising in dependence on name-and-form — then the whole vortex stops.

But this does not mean that the Arahant has no consciousness. The Arahant has consciousness that is *anidassana* — non-manifestative. It does not throw up signs. It does not construct a world. It is like a mirror that reflects nothing because there is nothing to reflect — not because the mirror is gone, but because the mirror is pure.

## Plain-English Notes

### Viññāṇaṃ Anidassanaṃ

Three qualities of the Arahant's consciousness:
- **Anidassana**: Non-manifestative. It doesn't throw up "things" to be known. Normal consciousness manifests — it lights up objects, creating a world of name-and-form. This consciousness doesn't.
- **Ananta**: Boundless. Not limited by identification with any "this" or "here."
- **Sabbato pabha**: Luminous on all sides. Not a beam from a source, but radiance without direction.

### The Four Elements Don't Get a Footing

The elements (earth, water, fire, air) are the building blocks of form. In this consciousness, they don't find a footing — they can't establish themselves. This means that the process of constructing form (rūpa) doesn't get started. No footing means no building.

### Long and Short, Fine and Coarse, Pleasant and Unpleasant

All pairs are held in check. Why? Because pairs require a standpoint. You need a "here" for "there" to exist. You need a "self" for "other" to exist. When consciousness no longer takes a stand, the entire paired structure collapses.

### The Dog and the Reflection

The dog sees its own reflection, thinks it's another dog, and snaps at it — losing its bone in the process. This is the worldling: seeing name-and-form, taking it as self, grasping at it, and losing everything.

The Arahant sees the reflection as reflection. No second dog. No bone to lose. No grasping.

### Emptiness and Signlessness as Pasture

The Arahant doesn't range in the world of signs (nimitta). He doesn't pick out features and construct realities. His range is emptiness — seeing everything as empty — and signlessness — not picking out signs at all. This is what it means to have Nibbāna as one's pasture.

## Meditations

### 1. The Mirror Meditation (Anidassana)

Sit with a mirror. Look at your reflection. Notice: it's not you. It's an image. It appears when you're in front of the mirror, disappears when you leave. It has no substance. But you can see it clearly.

Now close your eyes. Bring to mind the mirror of consciousness. Normally, it manifests — it shows you a world. Thoughts, sensations, perceptions — all reflections in the mirror.

Now ask: can there be a mirror that doesn't manifest anything? Not a blank mirror (which still shows the room), but a mirror that simply doesn't produce reflections? This is anidassana consciousness — consciousness that doesn't throw up signs.

Don't try to achieve it. Just consider the possibility. The verse says it exists. It's the Arahant's consciousness. Yours, for now, keeps manifesting. That's fine. Just knowing the difference is the beginning.

---

### 2. The Dog and the Bone Meditation (Seeing the Reflection)

Identify a belief you hold about yourself — "I am this kind of person," "I have this quality," "I am good/bad at this."

Now reflect: is this belief you, or is it a reflection? Like the dog seeing its own reflection in the water — the "other dog" is just the first dog reflected. Your belief about yourself is just name-and-form appearing in consciousness. It's not a second entity — it's your own reflection.

When the dog snaps at the reflection, it loses the bone. When you grasp at a self-belief, you lose the peace that's already there.

Practice: when a self-belief arises, don't snap at it. Don't grasp it. Don't reject it. Just see: "This is a reflection. This is name-and-form manifesting. It's not a self."

**The logic this reveals:** The "self" you're trying to protect or improve is a reflection of the very consciousness that's trying to protect it. Both are the same. The dog is chasing itself. When this is seen, the chasing stops.

---

### 3. The Boundless Meditation (Ananta)

Sit and notice the boundaries of your consciousness. It seems to be located "in here" — inside the head, behind the eyes. There's a boundary between what's "in here" and what's "out there."

Now imagine: this boundary is not a real boundary. It's a convention, a habit. Consciousness is not actually inside the body — the body is *in* consciousness. The sense of being inside is itself a manifestation of name-and-form.

Practice: gently relax the boundary. Don't locate yourself. Let the sense of "here" dissolve. If thoughts arise, don't place them inside or outside. Just let them be without location.

**The logic this reveals:** Ananta means boundless. The Arahant's consciousness is not bounded by identification with a body or a self. The sense of being "located" is a construction of name-and-form. When name-and-form is held in check, the boundary dissolves.

---

### 4. The Pasture Meditation (Emptiness and Signlessness)

What do you range in? What's the landscape of your mind? Notice: it's full of signs — "this is a thought," "this is a feeling," "this is me," "this is mine." You range in sign-land.

Now try: for one minute, don't pick out any signs. Don't identify anything. Don't label. Just let experience be undifferentiated. Don't notice "this" as distinct from "that."

You'll find it's almost impossible — the mind automatically picks out signs. That's what it does. But this experiment shows what signlessness (animitta) means: the mind no longer carves reality into separate, nameable things.

**The logic this reveals:** The Arahant's pasture is not a place. It's a mode of consciousness — one that doesn't carve the world into signs. Emptiness is not a void to look at but a way of seeing: everything as empty of self. This is where the Arahant ranges, not in the world of name-and-form.

---

## Summary of the Logic

Sermon 8 presents one of the most profound and difficult passages in the entire set — the verse from the Kevaḍḍhasutta:

1. **Viññāṇaṃ anidassanaṃ:** There is a consciousness that does not manifest name-and-form. It is non-manifestative, boundless, and luminous on all sides. In it, the four elements do not get a footing. All pairs are held in check.

2. **The dog and the reflection:** The worldling mistakes name-and-form for a self and grasps at it, like a dog snapping at its own reflection. The Arahant sees the reflection as reflection.

3. **The cessation of consciousness:** The last line says it is by the cessation of consciousness that all these are held in check. This is not annihilation — it is the cessation of the consciousness that arises dependent on name-and-form, the consciousness that manifests a world.

4. **The Arahant's pasture:** Emptiness and signlessness. Not a world of signs, not a world of name-and-form. The Arahant ranges in what is empty and signless — freedom itself.
