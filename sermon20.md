# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon XX

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Fire Simile (Aggivacchagottasutta)

When a fire goes out with the exhaustion of fuel, it is absurd to ask which direction it went. "Did it go east, west, north, or south?" The question is wrongly put. The fire does not "go" anywhere — it ceases because the conditions for its burning are exhausted. It comes to be reckoned simply as "gone out."

Likewise, the Tathāgata after death cannot be described as exists, not exists, both, or neither. The Tathāgata, having abandoned all identification with the five aggregates, is "deep, immeasurable, unfathomable as the great ocean." The tetralemma — the four-cornered logic of being and non-being — applies only to the conditioned, to what can be spoken of in terms of fuel. The Tathāgata is beyond all such reckonings.

### The Wind That Settles Nowhere

Consider the wind. It blows, it moves, it touches objects — but it settles nowhere. It is not established in any place. If you try to say "the wind is here" or "the wind is there," you miss its nature. The wind cannot be caught and held. It is known only in its passing.

The Tathāgata is like that wind. He is not established in form, not established in feeling, not established in perception, not established in preparations, not established in consciousness. He experiences these aggregates but does not abide in them. He does not stand on any of them as "I" or "mine." He has no footing in the conditioned world. The world tries to grasp him by the aggregates — "Is he this? Is he that?" — but he slips through every category. He is like the wind: you feel his presence in the world, but you cannot point to where he stands. He has ceased to stand anywhere at all.

### Tree-to-Tree Fire Spread

Imagine a forest fire spreading from one tree to another. The flame leaps across the gap, catches a new branch, burns, then leaps again. This is like craving passing from life to life — from one existence to the next, fuelled by ignorance and clinging. Each life is a new tree caught by the flame. The craving does not travel like a thing moving through space — it passes by way of conditionality, one existence providing the fuel for the next.

When the trees run out — when the fuel of grasping is exhausted — the fire stops. It does not go elsewhere. It simply ceases. The fetter that binds existence to existence (*bhava*) is cut at the root. There is no "where" for the fire to go because the conditions for its spread have been extinguished. The final life is the last tree in the forest — it burns, but nothing catches after it. The fire ends not because it has reached a destination, but because its fuel is gone.

### The Tetralemma and Its Limits

The tetralemma — the four-cornered logic — exhausts all possible positions that language can take regarding any particular existent. Either it is, or it is not, or both, or neither. The Buddha used this framework not to affirm one of the four corners but to show that the Tathāgata transcends all four.

If one says "the Tathāgata exists after death," that is reification — assuming a being that continues. If one says "he does not exist," that is annihilationism — assuming a being that ceases. If one says "both," that is a contradiction. If one says "neither," that is still a formulation in terms of being and non-being. All four presuppose that "the Tathāgata" is a referent that can be tracked across death. The Buddha cuts the root of all four by showing that the Tathāgata is not a referent at all.

### The Samiddhisutta Catechism

In the Samiddhisutta, a catechism unfolds between the Buddha and a monk named Samiddhi. The monk is asked: how does the world conceive of the Tathāgata? The answer reveals that the world conceives of the Tathāgata in terms of the five aggregates — as form, as feeling, as perception, as preparations, as consciousness. The world says "the Tathāgata is this body" or "the Tathāgata has this feeling" or "the Tathāgata is this mind."

But the Buddha declares that the Tathāgata is free from all such reckonings. He cannot be measured by any aggregate because he has broken the measuring rod of craving. The catechism shows that every attempt to pin down the Tathāgata is an attempt to grasp him through what he has abandoned. He is like the wind — felt but not held, known but not located.

The deeper point of the catechism is that the world's very method of knowing — "what is it? where is it? how is it?" — is itself a form of grasping. The world wants to classify the Tathāgata, to fit him into a category, to say "he is this kind of being." But the Tathāgata has abandoned all being. He is not a "what" to be classified. The catechism questions break down because the categories themselves do not apply.

## Plain-English Notes

### Aggivacchagottasutta

The sutta where the wanderer Vacchagotta asks the Buddha about the Tathāgata after death. The Buddha replies with the fire simile. The Tathāgata is like a fire that has gone out — the question of "where" does not apply.

The key insight: the fire is not "somewhere else" after going out. It has simply ceased. The Tathāgata is not transported to another plane at death — he is released from the very framework of planes and destinations.

### Tetralemma (Catukoṭika)

Four logical possibilities: X is, X is not, X both is and is not, X neither is nor is not. Nibbāna escapes all four because it is *asaṅkhata* — unconditioned. The tetralemma exhausts all possible positions within the conditioned realm, which is why the Buddha's silence is so significant: no verbal formulation can capture the unconditioned.

### The Five Aggregates (Pañcakkhandha)

Form, feeling, perception, preparations, consciousness. The Tathāgata is not found in any of these, nor apart from them. He is like the wind — not established anywhere. To seek the Tathāgata in the aggregates is to look for a fire in its ashes — the fuel is spent and the designation no longer applies.

The aggregates are the "domain of reckoning" — within them, the world classifies beings. The Tathāgata has stepped out of that domain entirely.

## Meditations

### The Extinguished Fire Contemplation

Take a question that troubles you: "What happens after death?" "Where do I go?" "Who am I really?" Notice the anxiety behind the question — the demand for an answer. Now consider the fire simile: when a fire goes out, where does it go? The question collapses.

Sit with the space left by the collapsed question. Do not replace it with another question. Let there be just the silence, the unfueled stillness.

**The logic this reveals:** The compulsion to ask "what happens after death" is itself a form of fuel — craving for continued existence in some form. Release the question, and the fire goes out.
