# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon XX

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Fire Embers Analogy

When no more firewood is added to a blazing fire, the flames subside and the logs already burning go on smouldering as embers. After some time, they too get extinguished and become ashes. This analogy illustrates the two Nibbāna elements (*dhātus*).

### Sa-upādisesā Nibbānadhātu

The arahant in his daily life is free from fresh graspings — lust, hate, and delusions do not flare up. But so long as he bears the burden of this organic combination, this physical frame, he has to experience certain afflictions and be receptive to likes and dislikes, pleasures and pains. In spite of all that, mentally he has access to the experience of the extinguishment he has already won. It is in that sense that the arahant is said to be in the Nibbāna element with residual clinging (*sa-upādisesā Nibbānadhātu*) in his everyday life, while taking in the objects of the five senses.

The "residual clinging" (*upādi* or *upādāna*) is not his own mental clinging — he has abandoned that. It is the organic body itself, which was "grasped" by past craving, that remains. Like the embers that still glow even when no new fuel is added, the arahant's body continues to function with its residual kammic momentum.

### Anupādisesā Nibbānadhātu

At the last moment of the arahant's life, even this organic body that had been grasped as *upādiṇṇa* is abandoned. It is at that moment that *anupādisesā Nibbānadhātu* — the Nibbāna element without residual clinging — comes in. The Nāgasutta gives a brief hint:

> *Vītarāga vītadoso vītamoho anāsavo, sarīraṃ vijahaṃ nāgo parinibbissati anāsavo.*
>
> "The one who has abandoned lust, hate, and delusion and is influx-free, that elephant of a man, on giving up his body, will attain full appeasement, being influx-free."

### Diṭṭhadhammika and Samparāyika

A more difficult problem concerns the sense in which these two are called *diṭṭhadhammika* (pertaining to this life) and *samparāyika* (pertaining to what comes after death). The sa-upādisesā Nibbānadhātu is diṭṭhadhammika — visible here and now, in this life, while the arahant is still alive. The anupādisesā Nibbānadhātu is samparāyika — it pertains to the moment after death, when the body is abandoned.

But both are aspects of one and the same Nibbāna. The extinguishment is not incomplete in the arahant's life — it is complete. What remains is the residual heat of the body, which at death becomes cool. The fire is out. The embers cool.

## Plain-English Notes

### Sa-upādisesā and Anupādisesā

| Term | Translation | Meaning |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Sa-upādisesā Nibbānadhātu | Nibbāna element with residual clinging | The arahant's experience while still alive, with the body remaining |
| Anupādisesā Nibbānadhātu | Nibbāna element without residual clinging | The arahant's final cessation at death, when the body is abandoned |

### Upādiṇṇa vs Upādāna

*Upādiṇṇa* means "grasped" — the body that was taken up by past craving. *Upādāna* means "grasping" or "fuel" — the activity of clinging. The arahant has abandoned upādāna but still bears upādiṇṇa (the body) until death.

### The Fire and Embers

The arahant's lust, hate, and delusion are extinguished like a flame that has run out of fuel. But the body still smoulders like embers — it feels pleasure and pain, experiences hunger and illness. At death, even the embers cool. The two Nibbāna elements are not two different Nibbānas but two stages of the same extinguishing.

## Meditations

### The Embers Contemplation

Think of something you crave — a desire that still burns in you. Now imagine that desire completely extinguished. No fuel. No fire. Coolness. But the body remains — you still breathe, eat, feel. That is sa-upādisesā — the fire is out, but the embers are warm.

Now consider death: when the body itself is gone, even the embers cool. That is anupādisesā. Not a new state. Just the complete cooling of what had already been extinguished.

Practice: notice a sensation of liking or disliking. See it as ember-heat. The arahant does not feed it. It cools on its own. Practice not adding fuel.

**The logic this reveals:** Nibbāna is not a future promise. The extinguishment is here, in the not-feeding of craving. What comes after death is just the body's cooling of what the mind has already cooled.
