# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 23

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Parable of the Raft

A man traveling a wilderness comes to a vast flood. The near shore is dangerous, the far shore is safe, but there is no boat and no bridge. Fearing for his safety, he gathers grass, twigs, branches, and leaves, binds them together, and builds a raft. By his effort — by his hands and feet — he crosses safely to the far shore. Now what should he do with the raft? If he hoists it onto his head or shoulders and carries it onward, he is a fool. The raft was for crossing, not for carrying. He leaves it on the shore and continues his journey.

The Dhamma is like that raft. It is for crossing over, not for clinging to. The four foundations of mindfulness, the noble eightfold path, the entire teaching — all of it is a means to an end. To grasp the Dhamma as "my doctrine" or "the only true way" is to hoist the raft onto one's head and stagger under its weight. The Buddha goes further: even good teachings must be abandoned, let alone bad ones. The ultimate teaching is not a teaching at all — it is the letting go of every support, every view, every certainty.

### The Snake Grasped by the Wrong End

If a man grasps a snake by the body, the snake turns back and bites him. But if he catches it skillfully with a forked stick, pressing down on the neck, he can handle it safely. So too with the Dhamma: grasped wrongly — as a set of propositions to defend, a badge of identity, a ground for pride over others — it turns and bites. The very teaching meant to free one from suffering becomes a new source of suffering.

This is *dhammapariyādāna* — holding the teaching as limited, as a possession rather than a tool. The Dhamma must be grasped correctly, as a means, not as an end in itself. The snake is for skillful handling; the raft is for crossing. Both are to be used and then released. There is no need to carry them further.

### Anupādā — The Essential Attitude

What is required is *anupādā* — non-clinging. Not merely non-clinging to the world of sense pleasures, but non-clinging even to the path itself. The Dhamma is like a ladder used to climb onto a roof: once you are on the roof, you leave the ladder behind. It would be a fool who, having climbed up, carries the ladder on his shoulder wherever he goes.

This principle extends even to Nibbāna. The highest attainment is not "I have attained Nibbāna" but the realization that there is no attainer. Even Nibbāna is not to be clung to — not because it is unworthy, but because clinging and Nibbāna are mutually exclusive. The raft was for crossing, not for carrying. When you have crossed, you leave all rafts behind — including the raft of the Dhamma itself.

## Plain-English Notes

### kulla (Raft)
The Dhamma as a raft: useful for crossing, burdensome when carried. This is the essential teaching of non-attachment applied to the teaching itself.

### alagaddūpama (Snake Simile)
Grasping the Dhamma wrongly is like grasping a snake by the wrong end — it turns and bites. The Dhamma must be grasped correctly, as a means, not as an end.

### upādāna (Clinging / Fuel)
The same word means "fuel" and "grasping." Clinging fuels the fire of becoming. It can cling to sensual pleasures, views, rules and vows, or self-doctrines — and even to the Dhamma itself.

### dhammapariyādāna (Holding the Teaching as Limited)
Treating the Dhamma as a finite set of doctrines to be defended rather than as a living path to be used and released.

### anupādā (Non-clinging)
The essential attitude: not grasping at anything, not even at Nibbāna. The highest attainment is not "I have attained" but "there is no attainer."

## Meditations

### Using the Raft Without Carrying It

Reflect on a teaching you cherish — perhaps one you defend or feel proud of understanding. Notice the sense of ownership, the subtle pride that says "I know this" or "I have realized this." Now imagine it as a raft of grass and twigs left on the shore. Can you honour it for having carried you across, yet let it rest where it served its purpose? The path is travelled by letting go, not by accumulating. Let the raft be on the shore.

### Letting Go of the Attainer

Sit quietly and observe the thought "I am meditating" or "I am practising." Notice how the sense of an attainer — someone who is making progress on the path — can itself become a subtle object of clinging. Now ask: if there were no attainer, would the practice still unfold? Let the self-concept dissolve into the background. The raft does not carry itself — it is used and abandoned. So too with the self that seems to be practising.

**The logic this reveals:** The purpose of the Dhamma is to eliminate grasping entirely, including any grasping of the Dhamma itself. Even good teachings must be abandoned. The highest realization is not "I have attained" but the stillness in which there is no attainer and no attained.
