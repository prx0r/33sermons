# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 23

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Parable of the Raft

A man traveling along a path comes to a great expanse of water. There is no boat, no bridge. Fearing for his safety, he gathers grass, wood, branches, and leaves, and builds a raft. He crosses over safely. Now, what should he do with the raft? Should he carry it on his head, hoist it on his shoulders, and continue his journey? No. He should leave it on the shore — it was built for crossing, not for carrying.

The Dhamma is like that raft. It is for crossing over, not for grasping. The teaching on the four foundations of mindfulness, the noble eightfold path — all of it is a means to an end. To cling to the Dhamma, to hold it as "my doctrine" or "the only true teaching," is to miss the point entirely.

### The Snake Simile

If a man catches a snake by the body, the snake may turn around and bite him. But if he catches it skillfully — by the neck with a forked stick — he can handle it safely. Similarly, the Dhamma, if grasped wrongly, bites back. One can become proud of one's learning, argumentative about one's interpretations, attached to one's views. The very teaching meant to free one from suffering becomes a new source of suffering.

This is *dhammapariyādāna* — holding the teaching as limited, as a possession rather than a tool. When the Dhamma is seen as a limited set of propositions to be defended, it is grasped by the wrong end.

### Anupādā — The Essential Attitude

What is required is *anupādā* — non-clinging. Not just non-clinging to the world, but non-clinging even to the path. The Dhamma is like a ladder used to climb onto a roof: once you are on the roof, you leave the ladder behind. It would be a fool who, having climbed up, says, "This ladder is so useful, I shall carry it with me wherever I go."

The raft is for crossing; the snake is for skillful handling; the ladder is for climbing. The Dhamma is for the cessation of suffering — not for building an identity.

## Plain-English Notes

### alagaddūpama (Snake Simile)
Grasping the Dhamma wrongly is like grasping a snake by the wrong end — it turns and bites.

### kulla (Raft)
The Dhamma as a raft: useful for crossing, burdensome when carried. This is the essential teaching of non-attachment applied to the teaching itself.

### upādāna (Clinging)
The fuel that sustains becoming. It can cling to sensual pleasures, views, rules and vows, or self-doctrines.

### dhammapariyādāna (Holding the Teaching as Limited)
Treating the Dhamma as a finite set of doctrines rather than as a living path to be used and released.

## Meditations

### Using the Raft Without Carrying It

Reflect on a teaching you cherish — perhaps one you defend or feel proud of understanding. Notice the sense of ownership. Now imagine it as a raft left on the shore. Can you honour it for having carried you across, yet let it rest where it served its purpose? The path is travelled by letting go, not by accumulating.

**The logic this reveals:** The purpose of the Dhamma is to eliminate grasping entirely, including any grasping of the Dhamma itself.
