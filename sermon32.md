# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 32

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### Sītibhūta — The Cooled One

The Arahant is described as "sītibhūta" — one who has become cool. The image is that of a fire that has gone out, its heat dissipated, its agitation stilled. Where the world burns with the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion, the Arahant is cool — not cold and indifferent, but free from the fever of craving.

This cooling is not a suppression of natural warmth. It is the natural state of a mind that has let go. The mind of the Arahant is like a lake in autumn — clear, calm, undisturbed. The ripples of craving no longer disturb its surface.

### Voices of Liberation — The Theragāthā and Therīgāthā

The verses of the Elder monks and nuns give us firsthand accounts of what this freedom feels like. These are not abstract philosophical statements — they are the spontaneous utterances of those who have seen the Deathless.

The Venerable Sāriputta declares:

> *Na me āsī, na me bhavissati, na me etarahi vijjati*
> "There was no 'mine,' there will be no 'mine,' there is no 'mine' now."

This is not a denial of experience but an absence of possessiveness. The Arahant relates to all phenomena without the sense of "I" or "mine."

The Venerable Aññākoṇḍañña, the first to understand the Dhamma, exclaims:

> *Bahunno vata tāṇena, nibbuto'mhi sakesu*
> "With much protection indeed, I have become cooled in my own (possessions)."

He sees that true protection is the relinquishment of the sense of ownership.

The Therī Soma, when told by Māra that women could not attain enlightenment, responds:

> *Itthibhāvaṃ kiṃ kayirā, cittamhi samāhite*
> "What does womanhood matter, when the mind is well-concentrated?"

The freedom of the Arahant transcends all gender, all identity, all social roles.

### Like the Moon Freed from the Clouds

The Theragāthā gives us a beautiful image:

> *Indo yathā pāṭaliphullam ambaraṃ, vimuttacitto'vasibhāvamajjhagā*
> "Like the moon freed from the clouds, the mind released has attained the state of no-more-becoming."

The moon does not struggle to be free of the clouds. When the clouds part, the moon simply shines. So too with the Arahant's mind — when the clouds of defilement are dispersed, the mind naturally shines with its own purity.

### The Arahant in Society

The Arahant still walks through the world. The Arahant still eats, sleeps, teaches, and responds to others. The difference is that there is no clinging behind any action. The Arahant acts from compassion, not from craving. The Arahant speaks from wisdom, not from attachment to views.

The Buddha himself lived among people for forty-five years after his enlightenment. He was not a recluse hiding from the world. He was fully in the world but not of it — like a lotus born in the mud but unsullied by the mud.

### Anāvila — Undisturbed

The Arahant's mind is anāvila — undisturbed, unruffled. In the midst of gain and loss, fame and disrepute, praise and blame, pleasure and pain, the mind remains steady. This is the eight worldly conditions that shake the world but cannot shake the Arahant.

The Arahant is described as "tādi" — "the such-like one." This word defies easy translation. The tādi is one who remains the same in all circumstances. Not because of indifference, but because of wisdom. The tādi sees that all conditioned things are impermanent, and so does not waver when they change.

## Plain-English Notes

### Sītibhūta
Become cool — the Arahant who has extinguished the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion. The fever of craving has subsided.

### Nibbuta
Extinguished — a synonym for Nibbāna, describing the complete going out of the fires of defilement.

### Vimutta
Released — the mind that is free from all bonds, all fetters, all limitations.

### Tādi
The such-like one — the Arahant who remains unshaken by the eight worldly conditions, the same in all circumstances.

## Meditations

### The Cooling Breath

Sit quietly and feel the natural coolness of the breath at the tip of the nose. With each inhalation and exhalation, imagine the heat of craving subsiding. Notice how the mind naturally cools when it stops reaching out for things. Rest in this coolness.

### Contemplating the Eight Worldly Conditions

Reflect on gain and loss, fame and disrepute, praise and blame, pleasure and pain. Notice how the mind is buffeted by these pairs. Practice remaining balanced — not pushing away the unpleasant nor pulling at the pleasant. This balanced mind is a taste of the Arahant's freedom.

**The logic this reveals:** True freedom is not the absence of contact with the world, but the absence of clinging in the midst of contact — like the moon that shines impartially on all beneath it, undisturbed by the clouds that pass across its face.
