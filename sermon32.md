# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 32

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### Sītibhūta — The Cooled One

Like a hot iron that has been plunged into water — *sītibhūta*, become cool. The fires of lust, hate, and delusion have been extinguished. The Arahant is "cooled." This is not a metaphor for indifference or emotional flatness. It is the natural state of a mind that has let go of its fever. The world burns with the three fires; the Arahant is the one who has stepped out of the furnace.

The cooling is total. Not a partial cooling, not a suppression that leaves residual heat, but a complete extinguishing. The hot iron hisses, the steam rises, and then — stillness. The iron is cool. It will never heat up again. The Arahant has seen the truth of suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path. That seeing has cooled the mind forever.

### Like the Moon Freed from Clouds

The Theragāthā gives us a beautiful image: "Like the moon freed from clouds, the mind is released, unattached, unbound." The moon does not struggle to be free of the clouds. It does not push them away. When the clouds disperse of their own accord, the moon simply shines. Its radiance was never diminished — only obscured.

So too with the Arahant's mind. The defilements are like clouds passing across the moon of awareness. The moon itself is pure. When the clouds of greed, hatred, and delusion disperse through wisdom, the mind shines with its natural radiance. The Arahant does not attain a new purity; the purity was always there, obscured by the clouds of clinging. The practice is not to brighten the moon but to let the clouds go.

### The Arahant in Society

The Arahant still walks, talks, eats, and teaches — but without clinging. The Buddha himself lived among people for forty-five years after his enlightenment, walking the dusty roads of India, eating whatever was offered, teaching whoever would listen.

Like a bird flying through the air — it leaves no trace. The bird does not grasp at the sky; the sky does not hold the bird. The Arahant moves through the world leaving no trace of craving, no footprint of attachment.

Like a bee collecting nectar from a flower — it takes what it needs without damaging the flower. The bee does not destroy the flower, does not cling to it, does not possess it. The Arahant takes what is needed for the sustenance of the body — food, clothing, shelter, medicine — without damaging, without clinging, without possessiveness.

### The Ājānīya Horse — The Thoroughbred

The Arahant is like a thoroughbred racehorse, an *ājānīya* horse. An ordinary horse runs because of the whip — it needs external restraint, external motivation. But a thoroughbred runs because of its own nature. It does not need the whip; it runs freely, gladly, with innate grace.

So too the Arahant. The ordinary person acts from fear — fear of punishment, fear of social disapproval, fear of kammic consequences. The Arahant acts from freedom. Virtue is not imposed from without; it flows from within. The sīla of the Arahant is spontaneous, effortless, natural. The thoroughbred does not need to be trained to run; running is its joy. The Arahant does not need to be trained to act rightly; right action is the natural expression of wisdom.

### Tādi — The Such-Like One

The Arahant is *tādi* — the Such-like One. Unmoved by praise or blame, gain or loss, fame or disrepute, pleasure or pain. Like a firm post at the city gate that stands unmoved no matter which way the wind blows. The eight worldly conditions — *aṭṭha lokadhamma* — shake the world but cannot shake the Arahant.

The tādi does not waver because the tādi has seen. When praise comes, it is seen as a conditioned phenomenon — pleasant, but impermanent, not-self. When blame comes, it is seen the same way. There is no one to be praised, no one to be blamed. The post at the city gate stands unmoved because it is firmly planted in the ground of wisdom.

### Voices of Liberation

The Theragāthā and Therīgāthā give us firsthand accounts. The Venerable Sāriputta declares: *Na me āsī, na me bhavissati, na me etarahi vijjati* — "There was no 'mine,' there will be no 'mine,' there is no 'mine' now." The Venerable Aññākoṇḍañña exclaims: *Bahunno vata tāṇena, nibbuto'mhi sakesu* — "With much protection indeed, I have become cooled in my own." The Therī Soma, told that women cannot attain enlightenment, responds: *Itthibhāvaṃ kiṃ kayirā, cittamhi samāhite?* — "What does womanhood matter, when the mind is well-concentrated?" The freedom of the Arahant transcends all identity, all gender, all social roles.

## Plain-English Notes

### Sītibhūta
Become cool — the Arahant who has extinguished the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion. The fever of craving has subsided entirely.

### Nibbuta
Extinguished — a synonym for Nibbāna, describing the complete going out of the fires of defilement.

### Vimutta
Released — the mind that is free from all bonds, all fetters, all limitations.

### Ājānīya
Thoroughbred — the Arahant who acts spontaneously from wisdom, not from external restraint or fear.

### Tādi
The Such-like One — the Arahant, unshaken by the eight worldly conditions, the same in all circumstances.

## Meditations

### The Cooling Practice

Sit quietly and feel the natural coolness of the breath at the tip of the nose. With each inhalation, imagine the heat of craving subsiding. With each exhalation, release the need to grasp. Rest in the coolness. Notice that this coolness is not produced; it is discovered.

### The Thoroughbred Reflection

Reflect on your own motivation. How much of your virtuous conduct comes from fear — fear of consequences, fear of judgement, fear of bad kamma? Can you act from wisdom instead? Can you be the thoroughbred who runs for the joy of running?

### The Firm Post Contemplation

When praise or blame comes, notice how the mind moves. Can you be like a firm post at the city gate? Not rigid, not indifferent, but firmly planted in the ground of seeing. The wind blows, the post stands. The praise comes, the mind knows it as conditioned. The blame comes, the mind knows it the same way.

### The Bird's Trace

At the end of the day, reflect: what traces have you left? Have you moved through the world like a bird flying — leaving no mark of craving, no imprint of possessiveness? Can you release the day without holding any of it?

**The logic this reveals:** True freedom is not the absence of contact with the world but the absence of clinging in the midst of contact. Like the moon that shines impartially on all beneath it, undisturbed by the clouds that pass across its face.
