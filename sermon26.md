# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 26

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### Dependent Arising as a Chain

*Paṭicca samuppāda* — dependent arising — is the Buddha's unique discovery, the insight that distinguishes His teaching from all others. It is the principle of conditionality: "When this is, that is; when this is not, that is not; from the arising of this comes the arising of that; from the cessation of this comes the cessation of that."

The twelve links form a chain: ignorance (*avijjā*) conditions preparations (*saṅkhārā*); preparations condition consciousness (*viññāṇa*); consciousness conditions name-and-form (*nāma-rūpa*); name-and-form conditions the six sense spheres (*saḷāyatana*); these condition contact (*phassa*); contact conditions feeling (*vedanā*); feeling conditions craving (*taṇhā*); craving conditions clinging (*upādāna*); clinging conditions becoming (*bhava*); becoming conditions birth (*jāti*); birth conditions decay-and-death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair. Like a row of dominoes, each link depends on the one before it. Knock the first, and the entire chain collapses in sequence. When ignorance ceases, the whole mass of suffering ceases. There is no mystery here — only the relentless logic of conditionality.

### Two Bundles of Reeds

The Buddha offers a striking image from the Saṃyutta Nikāya. Two bundles of reeds lean against each other — neither stands independently. Remove one, and the other falls. So it is with consciousness and name-and-form. They do not stand on their own — they lean on each other for support. Consciousness arises dependent on name-and-form; name-and-form is cognized dependent on consciousness. Neither is a self-standing entity. When one falls, both fall.

This image cuts through the illusion of a self at the deepest level. If neither bundle stands alone, there is nothing to call "me" or "mine" at the core of experience. The entire structure of existence is a leaning-together of conditioned phenomena — a mutual dependence with no independent essence anywhere. The self is like the space between the bundles: it seems real, but it is only the relationship itself.

### Fire Burning Dependent on Fuel

The fire does not come from the wood, nor from the match, nor from the air — it arises dependent on conditions. Take away any one of these conditions, and the fire cannot burn. The fire has no essence, no "fire-ness" that exists independently of its supporting conditions. It is a pattern of arising, not a thing.

So too with existence: it arises dependent on grasping. The fire of becoming does not come from a soul, a creator, or chance — it arises when the conditions of ignorance and craving are present. The whole of saṃsāra is a fire burning on the fuel of clinging. When the fuel of grasping is exhausted — when ignorance is seen through, craving is abandoned, and clinging is released — the fire of existence ceases. Not annihilation, but the end of burning. The chain collapses; the bundles fall; the fire goes out.

### Nibbāna Is Not a Link in the Chain

Some might ask: where does Nibbāna fit into the twelve links? The question misunderstands the nature of the teaching. Nibbāna is not a link — it is the cessation of the chain itself. It is not something that arises dependent on conditions — it is the stopping of the entire conditional process. Like a tree that has been uprooted: it no longer grows because its conditions — soil, water, sunlight — no longer feed it.

The noble eightfold path is the way to this cessation. Right view sees dependent arising; right intention inclines toward renunciation; right speech, action, and livelihood purify conduct; right effort, mindfulness, and concentration develop the mind. When these are cultivated, ignorance gradually yields to clear seeing — and the chain unwinds, link by link, until there is nothing left to bind.

## Plain-English Notes

### paṭicca samuppāda (Dependent Arising)
All phenomena arise in dependence on conditions. Like a row of dominoes — each link depends on the previous one. When ignorance ceases, the entire chain collapses.

### two bundles of reeds (naḷakalāpī)
Consciousness and name-and-form lean on each other like two bundles of reeds. Neither stands independently. Remove one, both fall.

### fire dependent on fuel
The fire does not come from the wood, the match, or the air — it arises dependent on conditions. So too existence arises dependent on grasping.

### twelve links
Ignorance → preparations → consciousness → name-and-form → six sense spheres → contact → feeling → craving → clinging → becoming → birth → decay-and-death.

### vibhava (Cessation)
Not annihilation, but the ceasing of conditioned phenomena when their supporting conditions are removed.

## Meditations

### Tracing the Chain Backwards

Sit with a moment of suffering — anger, restlessness, discontent, or longing. Trace it backwards through the chain: the suffering depends on birth (the sense "I am"), birth depends on becoming, becoming on clinging, clinging on craving, craving on feeling, feeling on contact, contact on the six sense spheres, these on name-and-form, name-and-form on consciousness, consciousness on preparations, preparations on ignorance. Now stop at ignorance. What is this ignorance? It is not knowing things as they are — the failure to see impermanence, suffering, and not-self. See directly into that not-knowing, and let the light of clear awareness shine. The first domino falls.

### Seeing the Bundles Fall

Contemplate the relationship between your awareness (consciousness) and your body-and-mind (name-and-form). Notice how they support each other: without a body, no awareness of the world; without awareness, no experience of the body. Neither stands alone. Feel the relief of not needing to find a self in either bundle. When both fall, what remains is not nothing — it is the ground that was always there, hidden by the leaning.

**The logic this reveals:** Suffering is not a brute fact — it is a chain of conditions. When the first condition (ignorance) is removed, the whole chain collapses, and Nibbāna reveals itself.
