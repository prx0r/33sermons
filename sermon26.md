# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 26

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Heart of Dependent Arising

*Paṭicca samuppāda* — dependent arising — is the Buddha's unique discovery, the insight that distinguishes His teaching from all others. It is the principle of conditionality: "When this is, that is; when this is not, that is not; from the arising of this comes the arising of that; from the cessation of this comes the cessation of that."

The chain of twelve links begins with ignorance (*avijjā*) and ends with the entire mass of suffering (*kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa*). But the chain is not a linear progression in time — it is a description of the structure of experience, here and now.

### The Links and Their Cessation

Ignorance conditions preparations (*saṅkhārā*); preparations condition consciousness (*viññāṇa*); consciousness conditions name-and-form (*nāma-rūpa*); name-and-form conditions the six sense spheres (*saḷāyatana*); the six sense spheres condition contact (*phassa*); contact conditions feeling (*vedanā*); feeling conditions craving (*taṇhā*); craving conditions clinging (*upādāna*); clinging conditions becoming (*bhava*); becoming conditions birth (*jāti*); and birth conditions decay-and-death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair.

Each link is a condition, not a cause in the absolute sense. And each link can be broken. The key is ignorance: when ignorance ceases, the entire chain collapses. But this is not a matter of acquiring knowledge — it is a matter of direct seeing.

### Nibbāna Is Not a Link in the Chain

Some might ask: where does Nibbāna fit into this chain? The question misunderstands the nature of the teaching. Nibbāna is not a link — it is the cessation of the chain itself. It is not something that arises dependent on conditions — it is the stopping of the whole conditional process.

When *vibhava* — cessation — is spoken of in the context of dependent arising, it is not annihilation. It is the cessation of the conditions that sustain suffering. Like a tree that has been uprooted: it no longer grows because its conditions — soil, water, sunlight — no longer feed it. So too with the mass of suffering: when its conditions are seen and abandoned, it ceases.

### The Path That Leads to Cessation

The noble eightfold path is itself the way to cessation. Right view sees dependent arising; right intention inclines toward renunciation; right speech, action, and livelihood purify conduct; right effort, mindfulness, and concentration develop the mind. When these are cultivated, ignorance gradually yields to clear seeing — and the chain unwinds.

## Plain-English Notes

### paṭicca samuppāda (Dependent Arising)
The principle that all phenomena arise in dependence on conditions. Nothing arises without cause, and nothing persists when its causes cease.

### vibhava (Cessation)
Not annihilation, but the ceasing of conditioned phenomena when their supporting conditions are removed.

### saṅkhārā (Preparations)
Volitional activities, mental formations, kammic forces. The link in dependent arising that conditions consciousness.

### viññāṇa (Consciousness)
Not a transmigrating soul, but a process that arises dependent on conditions. It is the awareness of an object — nothing more.

## Meditations

### Tracing the Chain Backwards

Sit with a moment of suffering — perhaps a feeling of anger or restlessness. Trace it backwards: the suffering depends on birth (the sense "I am"), birth depends on becoming, becoming on clinging, clinging on craving, craving on feeling, feeling on contact, contact on the six sense spheres, the six sense spheres on name-and-form, name-and-form on consciousness, consciousness on preparations, preparations on ignorance. Now stop at ignorance. What is this ignorance? It is not knowing things as they are — it is the failure to see impermanence, suffering, and not-self. See directly into that not-knowing, and let the light of awareness shine.

**The logic this reveals:** Suffering is not a brute fact — it is a chain of conditions. When the first condition (ignorance) is removed, the whole chain collapses, and Nibbāna reveals itself.
