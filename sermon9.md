# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 09

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*

---

## The Teaching

### The Extended Passage on Anidassana Viññāṇa

The Brahmanimantanikasutta of the Majjhima Nikāya gives us an extended version of the anidassana viññāṇa passage. The Buddha uttered this passage to break the conceit of Baka the Brahma, who believed himself to be permanent and unchanging:

> *Viññāṇaṃ anidassanaṃ anantaṃ sabbato pabhaṃ, taṃ paṭhaviyā paṭhavittena ananubhūtaṃ, āpassa āpattena ananubhūtaṃ, tejassa tejattena ananubhūtaṃ, vāyassa vāyattena ananubhūtaṃ... sabbassa sabbattena ananubhūtaṃ.*
>
> "Consciousness which makes nothing manifest, infinite and all lustrous. It does not partake of the earthiness of earth, the wateriness of water, the fieriness of fire, the airiness of air... the all-ness of the all."

The Buddha declares: "I have directly known earth without partaking of earthiness, water without partaking of wateriness..." This is the knowledge that Baka the Brahma lacked. He identified with his Brahma-existence, taking its "Brahma-ness" as real and permanent. But the Buddha's consciousness does not partake of any such essence — not Brahma-hood, not deva-hood, not even all-ness.

### The Arahant's Proclamation of Voidness

There are two verses that express the Arahant's liberation in terms of this non-manifestative consciousness:

The Arahant lets go before Māra comes to grab. He does not hold on to anything that Māra could seize. The thought "I was" does not occur. The idea "I am" is not present. Nor does the idea "I will be" arise.

This is the Arahant's proclamation of voidness: no past self, no present self, no future self. The consciousness that would hold these thoughts does not arise. The Arahant has given up all three times — he has gone beyond the "was," "am," and "will be."

> *Viññāṇaṃ anidassanaṃ anantaṃ sabbato pabhaṃ, taṃ paṭhaviyā paṭhavittena ananubhūtaṃ, āpassa āpattena ananubhūtaṃ, tejassa tejattena ananubhūtaṃ, vāyassa vāyattena ananubhūtaṃ... sabbassa sabbattena ananubhūtaṃ.*
>
> "Consciousness which makes nothing manifest, infinite and all lustrous. It does not partake of the earthiness of earth, the wateriness of water, the fieriness of fire, the airiness of air... the all-ness of the all."

The gist of this paragraph is that the non-manifestative consciousness which is infinite and all lustrous, is free from the qualities associated with any of the concepts in the list. It is not under their influence, it does not partake of them — *ananubhūtaṃ*. Whatever nature the world attributes to these concepts, whatever reality they invest it with, that is not registered in this non-manifestative consciousness.

Usually, the worldlings attribute a certain degree of reality to concepts in everyday usage. These may be reckoned as mind-objects, things that the mind attends to. The worldling thinks that there is something, some inherent nature or essence, in each of these objects of the mind. But this consciousness does not partake of that supposed essence.

The word *ananubhūtaṃ* is constructed with *anu* and *bhū*. One might ordinarily understand *anubhava* as "experience." But here it carries the sense of something that is "become after" or "taking after" — being influenced by, taking on the quality of. The Arahant's consciousness does not take after anything. Earthiness does not make it earthy. Wateriness does not make it watery. The concepts have no power over it.

### The Dumb Show: A Drama Without Props

An insight meditator goes through an experience similar to a mime or a dumb show when he contemplates on name-and-form, seeing the four elements as empty and void of essence.

In a dumb show, one might see such acts as follows: An actor rides a no-bike, climbs a no-hill, meets a no-friend and has a no-chat with him. Or else he may sit on a no-chair by a no-table and writes a no-letter with a no-pen. On the stage there is neither a bicycle, nor a hill, nor another person, nor any other object like a chair, a table or a pen. All these are merely suggested by his acting.

An insight meditator, too, goes through a similar experience. He feels as if he is performing a drama with non-existing objects. He opens a non-existing door, sits on a non-existing chair. The four elements begin to fade away for him, and the factors in the name group assume a ghostly character. The solidity of experience dissolves.

When a meditator becomes dispassionate with regard to these four elements, when they begin to fade away for him, he realizes that — as in the case of the dumb show — he is involved with things that do not really exist. This amounts to an understanding that the factors of the name group are dependent on the form group, and vice versa. Both are void of essence.

What is essential here is the very understanding of essencelessness. If one sits down to draw up lists of concepts and prescribe them, it would only lead to a mental constipation. Instead of release there will be entanglement.

### Not Partaking of the All-ness of All

The passage extends all the way up to "the all-ness of the all" — *sabbassa sabbattena ananubhūtaṃ*. The "all" (sabba) in the Buddha's teaching refers to the six sense bases and their objects — the entirety of what can be experienced. The Arahant's consciousness does not even partake of "all-ness." It does not appropriate the entire field of experience as "everything."

This non-manifestative consciousness is not a consciousness *of* anything. It does not take any object. It does not establish itself on any footing. It is the consciousness that is no longer part of the reciprocal vortex of consciousness and name-and-form. When the vortex stops, what remains is not nothing — it is consciousness that is anidassana, ananta, sabbato pabha.

## Plain-English Notes

### Ananubhūtaṃ (Not Partaking Of)

The Arahant's consciousness does not "take after" anything. Earthiness does not impress it. It does not become earthy by contact with earth. The things that influence ordinary consciousness — the solidity of matter, the fluidity of water, the heat of fire, the movement of air — these do not get a footing in this consciousness. They pass through without leaving a trace.

### The Dumb Show

A mime suggests a wall by pressing against it — but there's no wall. He rides a bike that isn't there. The meditator on the path to Arahant-hood begins to see ordinary experience the same way: the solid world is suggested but not actually there. The four elements are like the mime's props — they appear to be there but are empty.

### The All-ness of the All

The "all" (sabba) is eye and forms, ear and sounds, nose and smells, tongue and tastes, body and touches, mind and mind-objects. The Arahant's consciousness does not even partake of "all-ness" — it does not take the totality of experience as a thing. Even the sense of "everything" is not registered.

## Meditations

### 1. The Dumb Show Meditation

Sit and look at your hand. It seems solid, real, substantial. Now imagine: this solidity is a suggestion, like a mime suggesting a wall. The elements (earth, water, fire, air) are no-things that the mind has made into things.

Try to see your hand the way you'd see a mime's imaginary wall — it's "there" but not really there. The solidity is a suggestion, a convention. The thingness of the hand is a no-thingness.

**The logic this reveals:** The meditator begins to see that the solid world is a dumb show. The props aren't real — they're suggested by the interplay of name-and-form.

---

### 2. The Not-Partaking Meditation

Pick a sense object — a sound, a sight, a touch. Notice how consciousness "takes after" it. When you hear a bird, consciousness becomes bird-ish. When you feel cold, consciousness becomes cold-ish.

Now consider: consciousness that is anidassana does not take after anything. It is like a mirror that does not become the things it reflects. The mirror remains mirror, unaffected by the images.

Practice: for a few minutes, try to receive experience without letting consciousness take after it. Hear without becoming the hearing. Feel without becoming the feeling.

**The logic this reveals:** The Arahant's consciousness is ananubhūtaṃ — it does not partake. Earthiness, wateriness, all-ness — none of these qualities register. The consciousness remains itself, luminous, boundless, and free.

---

## Summary of the Logic

Sermon 9 deepens the analysis of anidassana viññāṇa from the Brahmanimantanikasutta:

1. **Ananubhūtaṃ:** The Arahant's consciousness does not partake of or take after any quality — not earthiness, not wateriness, not even the all-ness of all. It is uninfluenced, unimpressed.

2. **The dumb show:** The meditator sees the four elements as empty, like a mime's non-existent props. The drama of existence is seen as a drama. Name-and-form are mutually dependent and both essenceless.

3. **A consciousness without an object:** Anidassana consciousness does not manifest anything. It is not consciousness *of* something. It is the consciousness of one who has stopped the vortex.
