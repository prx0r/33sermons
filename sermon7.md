# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 07

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*

---

## The Teaching

### The Pre-Buddhistic Dilemma: The Limit of Tranquillity

The pre-Buddhistic ascetics, and even Āḷāra Kālāma and Uddaka Rāmaputta, thought that they can get out of saṃsāra by tranquillizing the bodily activities, the verbal activities, and the mental activities. But they did not understand that all these are saṅkhāras, or preparations, therefore they were confronted with a certain dilemma. They went on calming down the bodily activities to subtler and subtler levels. They calmed down the in-breaths and out-breaths, they managed to suppress thinking and pondering by concentration exercises, but without proper understanding. It was only a temporary calming down.

However, once they reached the level of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, they had to face a certain problem. In fact, the very designation of that level of attainment betrays the dilemma they were in. It means that one is at a loss to say definitely whether there is some perception or not. The Pañcattayasutta clearly reveals this fact:

> *Saññā rogo saññā gaṇḍo saññā sallaṃ, asaññā sammoho, etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ nevasaññānāsaññaṃ.*
>
> "Perception is a disease, perception is a boil, perception is a dart, but not to have perception is to be deluded. This is peaceful, this is excellent, that is, neither-perception-nor-non-perception."

They understood to some extent that perception is a disease, a trouble, a tumour, or a wound, or else a thorn. They wanted to be free from perception. But then, on the other hand, they feared that to be totally free from perception is to be in a deluded state. Therefore they concluded: "This is peaceful, this is excellent, that is neither-perception-nor-non-perception," and came to a halt there. That is why the Buddha rejected even Āḷāra Kālāma and Uddaka Rāmaputta and went in search of the stilling of all preparations.

So the kind of tranquillity meditation followed by the pre-Buddhistic ascetics, through various higher knowledges and meditative attainments, could never bring about a stilling of all preparations. Why? Because the ignorance underlying those preparations were not discernible to their level of wisdom. In the least, they could not even recognize their saṅkhāra nature.

---

### The Iceberg of Ignorance

That activity is something internal. But even at that level, defilements lie dormant, because ignorance is hiding there too. In fact, that is precisely why they are reckoned as saṅkhāra. Usually, one thinks in terms of 'I' and 'mine', as: "I breathe," "I speak," "I see," and "I feel." So, like the submerged portion of an iceberg, these subtler layers of preparations also have ignorance hidden within them. That is why the attempt of pre-Buddhistic ascetics to solve the saṃsāric riddle by tranquillity alone met with failure.

The stilling of all preparations — *sabbasaṅkhārasamatha* — is not achieved by suppressing them to subtler levels. It is achieved by understanding their nature as preparations, as dependently arisen, as empty of a self. The ignorance that hides in the submerged portion of the iceberg must be illuminated by wisdom.

---

### Suffering Is Dependently Arisen

In one discourse in the Nidānasaṃyutta of the Saṃyutta Nikāya we find the Buddha making the following significant statement:

> *Paṭiccasamuppannaṃ kho, Upavāṇa, dukkhaṃ. Kiṃ paṭicca? Phassaṃ paṭicca.*
>
> "Upavāṇa, I have declared that suffering is dependently arisen. Dependent on what? Dependent on contact."

So from this statement, also, it is clear that the five groups of grasping arise because of contact, that is by contacting through the six bases. Considered in this way, the five grasping groups are suffering — *saṅkhittena pañcupādānakkhandhā dukkhā*. So suffering is something dependently arisen.

Now what is suffering in the ultimate sense? It is these five grasping groups themselves. But they are dependently arisen. That means they are not self-existent. They depend on contact. And contact depends on the six sense-bases. And the six sense-bases depend on name-and-form. And so on, back through the chain.

But the practical point is this: suffering is not something that happens *to* you — it's what you *are* when you grasp at the aggregates. And it arises here and now through contact, not through some past cause that you need to trace back lifetimes.

### The Back-Stage of the Drama

The relation of saṅkhāras to ignorance is somewhat similar to the relation a drama has to its back-stage preparations. The drama appears real only so long as the back-stage preparations remain hidden. Similarly, saṅkhāras appear as a real self, as real activities of a real person, only so long as ignorance hides their true nature.

In a drama, if the audience could see the back-stage — the actors changing costumes, the stagehands moving props, the director giving instructions — the illusion would be broken. The drama would cease to be a drama; it would be seen as a preparation. Similarly, when wisdom illuminates the back-stage of experience — the breathing that prepares the body, the thinking that prepares speech, the perceiving that prepares mind — saṅkhāras are seen as preparations, not as a self.

---

### The Significance of Saṅkhāra

The relation of saṅkhāras to ignorance is somewhat similar to the relation a drama has to its back-stage preparations. The drama appears real only so long as the back-stage preparations remain hidden. Similarly, saṅkhāras appear as a real self, as real activities of a real person, only so long as ignorance hides their true nature.

When ignorance is removed by wisdom, saṅkhāras are seen for what they are: mere preparations, mere activities, with no self behind them. The stilling of all preparations is not the stopping of activity — it is the stopping of the ignorance that makes activity appear as self.

## Plain-English Notes

### The Pre-Buddhistic Ceiling

Āḷāra Kālāma and Uddaka Rāmaputta were the Buddha's first two teachers. They taught him to attain the formless jhānas — the sphere of nothingness and the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. The Buddha mastered these and then left, because they did not lead to the stilling of all preparations.

Why? Because they suppressed saṅkhārā without understanding them. The perception becomes so subtle it's neither present nor absent. But the ignorance is still there — just submerged, like the bottom of an iceberg. You can't see it, but it's still driving the whole thing.

The Buddha's discovery: the stilling of all preparations comes not from making them subtler, but from seeing them clearly. Insight, not tranquillity alone, uproots ignorance.

### Saññā as Disease

The pre-Buddhistic ascetics saw perception as a disease, a boil, a dart. They weren't wrong about that. But their solution was to escape perception. The Buddha's solution was to understand perception. The difference between suppression and understanding is the difference between temporary peace and final Nibbāna.

### The "Not-Self" in Preparations

When you say "I breathe," you're adding a self to a preparation. Breathing is a preparation (kāyasaṅkhāra). The "I" is an extra layer — ignorance compressing the activity into a unit called "me." The stilling of all preparations means seeing the breathing as breathing, the thinking as thinking, the feeling as feeling — without adding the "I."

## Meditations

### 1. The Ceiling Meditation (Seeing the Limit of Tranquillity)

Sit and calm the mind. Let the breath become subtle. Let thoughts settle. Enjoy the stillness.

Now ask: is this stillness the end? Or is there still a subtle "I" enjoying the stillness? Is there still a background of ignorance — a sense that "I am still, I am peaceful"?

The pre-Buddhistic ascetics stopped here. They mistook the tranquillity for the goal. But the Buddha saw that even this subtle "I am" is a preparation.

Practice: find the "I am" at the most subtle level of your meditation. Don't suppress it. See it as a preparation. See it as dependently arisen. See it as not-self.

**The logic this reveals:** Tranquillity alone doesn't uproot ignorance. It only makes ignorance subtler. The stilling of all preparations is a matter of seeing, not calming.

---

### 2. The Iceberg Meditation (Finding the Submerged Ignorance)

The iceberg metaphor: most of the mass is below the surface. Your conscious mind is the tip. Below: breathing, subtle thinking, automatic perception, latent tendencies.

Sit and try to sense the submerged part:
- Feel the breath happening without "you" doing it
- Notice how perception occurs before you name it
- Feel the automatic pleasant/unpleasant/neutral assessment of every experience

Now ask: is there a subtle "I" in all of this? Even the sense of "I am watching" — is that another preparation?

**The logic this reveals:** Ignorance hides in the subtlest levels. The "I" that watches is also a preparation. When you see this, even the watcher dissolves.

---

### 3. The Contact Meditation (Seeing Suffering Arise)

The Buddha told Upavāṇa: suffering is dependently arisen — dependent on contact.

Sit and notice contact happening: eye meets form, ear meets sound, nose meets smell, tongue meets taste, body meets touch, mind meets thought.

Now notice: with each contact, feeling arises. With feeling, craving stirs. With craving, grasping. This is the arising of suffering — visible in real time.

Now notice: without trying to stop contact, can you see it as just contact? Not "I am seeing" but "seeing is occurring." Not "I hear" but "hearing." When the "I" is removed from contact, the chain breaks.

**The logic this reveals:** You don't need to stop contact to end suffering. You need to see contact as contact — empty, dependently arisen, not-self. The pre-Buddhistic ascetics tried to stop contact. The Buddha taught to see through it.

---

## Summary of the Logic

Sermon 7 shows the *limit* of the pre-Buddhistic path and the *distinctiveness* of the Buddha's discovery:

1. **The tranquillity ceiling:** Suppressing saṅkhārā without understanding them leads to a dead end. The formless attainments are peaceful but not Nibbāna.

2. **The iceberg:** Ignorance hides in the submerged portion of saṅkhārā — in the breathing, the subtle thinking, the automatic perception. Tranquillity doesn't reach there.

3. **Suffering is dependently arisen — dependent on contact:** Not on some primordial cause, but on the simple meeting of sense and object, here and now. The cessation of suffering is the cessation of that dependence.

4. **The stilling of all preparations:** Not suppression but seeing. When preparations are seen as preparations — empty, dependently arisen, not-self — they are stilled. Not by force, but by understanding.
