# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon XXI

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Arahant's Mind Like a Cloudless Sky

The Arahant's mind is like a cloudless sky. The six sense spheres still operate — the eye sees forms, the ear hears sounds, the nose smells odours, the tongue tastes flavours, the body feels tangibles, the mind cognises thoughts. All of this continues. But just as birds fly across a cloudless sky leaving no trace, sense-objects touch the Arahant's mind without leaving traces of craving.

There is no passion (*rāga*) upon contact with a pleasant object. There is no aversion (*dosa*) upon contact with a painful object. There is no delusion (*moha*) about the nature of what is experienced. The sky does not say "a bird passed" or "no bird passed." It is simply sky. So too the Arahant's mind — luminous, empty, uncompounded. Seeing occurs, hearing occurs, but there is no "one who sees" or "one who hears" apart from the bare process. The sense-spheres function without the overlay of "I" or "mine."

### Fire Rekindling vs Full Extinction

Consider a fire that has died down. The flames are gone, but the coals are still hot. If a wind blows, the embers can rekindle — a new flame can catch, the fire can resume. This is like the ordinary worldling: the fires of passion may subside temporarily, but the underlying tendency is still there. Given the right conditions — a tempting sight, a seductive sound — the fire flares up again. The coals of latent tendency (*anusaya*) are still warm beneath the surface.

But the Arahant is like a fire so completely out that no wind can rekindle it. The fuel of craving has been exhausted entirely. The body is the residual coals — it still gives off warmth, it still experiences pleasure and pain — but there is no possibility of the fires of defilement re-igniting. The root has been destroyed. The embers are dying, not smouldering with potential. Even the most provocative sense-object, the most challenging circumstance, cannot reignite what has been utterly extinguished. This is the difference between temporary suppression and final liberation.

The contrast is crucial: the worldling's mind is a fire that can be rekindled at any moment. The Arahant's mind is a fire that has gone beyond the possibility of rekindling. No amount of wind — no provocation, no temptation, no circumstance — can reignite what has been fully extinguished.

### The Body as Residual Fuel

The body itself is the "residual clinging" (*upādiṇṇa*). It was "clung-to" by past craving acting through kamma. The Arahant still bears it, but does not grasp it anew. He does not claim ownership over it.

This distinction is subtle but critical. The ordinary person experiences the body as "me" or "mine." When the body is sick, they are sick. When the body ages, they despair. When the body dies, they are terrified. The Arahant experiences the body as a process — a flow of elements sustained by past conditions. He attends to its needs as one attends to a rented room, but he does not build a sense of self around it.

This is like carrying a borrowed item. You use a borrowed umbrella, a borrowed coat — you wear it, you benefit from it, but you do not claim it as your own. When the owner asks for it back, you return it without regret. The Arahant carries the body in this way. He experiences its pleasures and pains, its health and illness, its youth and aging — but he does not identify with any of it. He does not say "this is mine" or "this is I" or "this is my self." The borrowed item is used without possessiveness. At death, it is returned — the body breaks up, and there is no further taking up of any existence.

The borrowed-item simile also illuminates the relationship between the Arahant and the world. Just as one who borrows a coat does not boast about its quality or feel ashamed of its wear, the Arahant does not become elated by pleasant experiences or depressed by painful ones. The body is on loan from conditions — it will be returned when conditions conspire. There is no anxiety about its upkeep, no obsession with its preservation, no fear of its dissolution.

## Plain-English Notes

### Upādiṇṇa — "Clung-to"

The body is called "clung-to" because it is the result of past grasping. It is residual fuel — not new fuel, but the last of the old fuel still burning.

This is why the Arahant is not entirely free from bodily feeling while alive. The embers are still warm. But he is free from the *reaction* to feeling — the grasping that turns mere sensation into suffering.

### Phassa (Contact) and Vedanā (Feeling)

For the Arahant, contact and feeling occur but do not lead to craving. The gap between feeling and grasping has been seen through. Feeling arises and ceases like a bubble on water — no trace left behind.

### Sa-upādisesa vs Anupādisesa — A Summary

The two elements are not degrees of Nibbāna; they are the same extinguishment viewed under two conditions. In sa-upādisesa, the body remains as residual heat. In anupādisesa, even that residual heat has subsided.

### The Two Nibbāna Elements — Further Clarified

| Aspect | Sa-upādisesā | Anupādisesā |
|--------|--------------|--------------|
| Body | Present, residual fuel | Abandoned |
| Defilements | Already extinguished | Already extinguished |
| Rekindling | Impossible | Impossible |
| Phase | Living Arahant | After death |

The body is the only variable between the two. The defilements are equally extinct in both. This is why Nibbāna is said to be "visible here and now" (*diṭṭhadhamma*) — the extinguishment is already complete in the living Arahant. Death adds nothing to the realization; it merely removes the last residue.

## Meditations

### The Borrowed Body Contemplation

Sit quietly and notice the body. Feel its weight, its warmth, its breathing. Now reflect: this body was not chosen by you. It was given by past conditions — kamma, craving, ignorance. It is on loan.

You use it to see, to hear, to move, to think. But can you truly call it yours? When illness comes, can you refuse it? When aging comes, can you stop it? The body does what conditions dictate, not what "you" command. See the body as a borrowed garment — useful, but not owned. At death, it is returned. There is no "owner" to be found.

**The logic this reveals:** The sense of ownership over the body is an added fabrication. Let it go, and what remains is just the natural process — arising and ceasing, without a claimant.
