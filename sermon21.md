# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 21

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Two Nibbāna Elements and the Arahant's Daily Life

We have spoken of the two Nibbāna elements — *sa-upādisesa* and *anupādisesa*. The first is with residual clinging, the second without. What is this residual clinging? It is the body itself, this *upādiṇṇa* aggregate — the "clung-to" body born of past grasping.

The Arahant still experiences *phassa* — contact through the six sense spheres. The eye sees forms, the ear hears sounds, the nose smells odours, the tongue tastes flavours, the body feels tangibles, the mind cognises thoughts. But nowhere in this experience is there *taṇhā*. The contact occurs, feeling (*vedanā*) arises — pleasant, painful, or neutral — but the mind does not grasp. It is like rain falling on a cloudless sky: the drops touch nothing that holds them.

### The Smouldering Embers Simile Continued

When a fire has consumed its fuel and only embers remain, it is still hot — it can still burn if fuel is given. But if no fuel is offered, the embers cool. The Arahant's body is like those embers. It still experiences pleasure and pain, but there is no fuel of craving to feed the fire of *bhava* — becoming. The body lives out its remaining term, sustained by the momentum of past clinging, but no new fuel is added.

When the body breaks up at death, even this residual fuel is gone. That is the *anupādisesa* Nibbāna — complete cessation without remainder, like a fire that has gone out because every last ember has cooled.

### The Cloudless Sky

The Arahant's mind is like a cloudless sky. Clouds arise and pass — these are the sense impressions, the thoughts, the feelings. But they do not stain the sky. The sky does not say "I am clear" or "I am stained." It simply is what it is — luminous, empty, uncompounded. So too the Arahant's mind: the six sense spheres function, but without passion (*rāga*), aversion (*dosa*), or delusion (*moha*). The defilements have been cut at the root.

## Plain-English Notes

### upādiṇṇa (Clung-to)
The body is called "clung-to" because it is the result of past grasping. It is the residual fuel that keeps the Arahant in the world until *parinibbāna*.

### phassa (Contact)
The meeting of sense organ, object, and consciousness. For the Arahant, contact occurs but does not give rise to craving.

### vedanā (Feeling)
Pleasant, painful, or neutral feeling. In the Arahant, feeling is felt without the overlay of "I-feel" or "mine." It arises and ceases like a bubble on water.

### sa-upādisesa / anupādisesa
"With residual fuel" and "without residual fuel" — the two aspects of Nibbāna, referring to the Arahant's experience while alive and at death.

## Meditations

### Seeing Feeling Without Grasping

Sit quietly and attend to the senses. When a sound is heard, notice the bare fact of hearing. Is there a "hearer" apart from the hearing? When a pleasant feeling arises, watch it closely — does it demand to be held? When a painful feeling arises, does it demand to be pushed away? Observe the gap between contact and grasping. That gap is the Arahant's constant abode.

**The logic this reveals:** Grasping is not inherent in feeling — it is an added layer of mental activity that can be seen through and abandoned.
