# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 25

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Senses on Fire

The Ādittapariyāya — the Fire Sermon — is one of the most famous discourses of the Buddha. "All is burning," He declared to the thousand bhikkhus at Gayāsīsa. The eye is burning, forms are burning, eye-consciousness is burning, eye-contact is burning, and whatever feeling arises with that contact — whether pleasant, painful, or neutral — is burning. All are burning with the fire of lust, the fire of hate, the fire of delusion, burning with birth, decay, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair.

This is not poetry. It is a precise diagnosis. Every sense door is a furnace. Every contact is fuel. The worldling lives inside a bonfire and calls it life.

### The Man with Leprosy

The Māgaṇḍiyasutta offers a haunting companion image. A man with leprous skin — his flesh raw, suppurating, and itching — sits beside a pit of hot embers. He scorches himself, and the heat brings temporary relief from the itch. He feels pleasure in the burning. He calls it happiness. But the disease is still there — the leprosy has not healed. The relief is a deception.

When that man's skin heals — when he becomes healthy — he will look back at that pit of embers with horror. What he once called pleasure, he now sees as agony. The worldling is like the leper: he warms himself at the fire of sense pleasures and mistakes the heat for happiness. The Arahant is the healthy man who sees sense pleasures for what they are — a fire that consumes without ever truly satisfying. The cure is not a better fire, but the healing of the disease of craving itself.

### The Extinction Is Not Annihilation

When a fire goes out because its fuel is consumed, where does it go? This question has haunted philosophers for millennia. The Buddha turns it around: the question assumes that the fire was a thing, a substance, an entity that could travel somewhere. But fire is a process — a phenomenon of burning dependent on conditions. When the conditions are removed, the process ceases. It does not "go" anywhere, because it was never a "thing" that could travel.

So too with the Arahant. The five aggregates still function during life — the body experiences, the mind cognises — but there is no fuel of craving to sustain the fire of becoming. At death, the process ceases utterly. Not annihilation — for annihilation requires a self to be annihilated. The Arahant does not "not exist"; he simply no longer burns. The fire has gone out for lack of fuel.

### Upādāna — Fuel and Grasping

The word *upādāna* means both "fuel" and "clinging." This is no accident of language — it is a profound insight into the nature of existence. Firewood is fuel that the fire "grasps" to sustain itself. Clinging is the fuel for existence. The eye grasps at forms, the ear at sounds, the mind at ideas. Where there is grasping, there is burning. Where there is no grasping, the fire ceases.

The three fires are *rāga* (lust), *dosa* (hate), and *moha* (delusion). They burn because they feed on objects. When the mind sees the danger in these fires, it withdraws the fuel of enjoyment (*assāda*), sees the danger (*ādīnava*), and finds freedom (*nissaraṇa*). Nibbāna is literally the extinguishing of these fires. Remove the fuel, and the fire stops. What remains is the cool, the stillness, the peace that words cannot convey.

## Plain-English Notes

### āditta (Burning)
The eye, forms, eye-consciousness, eye-contact, and feeling — all burning with the fires of lust, hate, delusion, birth, decay, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, despair.

### upādāna (Fuel / Grasping)
The same word means both. Firewood is "fuel" that the fire "grasps." Clinging is the fuel for existence. Remove the fuel, the fire stops.

### nibbāna (Extinction)
Literally "going out," as a fire goes out. Not annihilation — the fire does not "go" anywhere — but the cessation of the burning of defilement.

### leper simile (Māgaṇḍiyasutta)
The leper scorching himself at a pit of embers, mistaking the heat for relief. The worldling does the same with sense pleasures. The Arahant is the healthy man who sees the fire as it is.

### rāga, dosa, moha
Lust, hate, and delusion — the three root defilements. Their complete eradication is Nibbāna.

## Meditations

### Feeling the Fire Go Out

Sit with a pleasant sensation — perhaps the warmth of the sun, a comfortable posture, or a pleasant memory. Notice the subtle wanting, the desire for the pleasant feeling to continue or intensify. That wanting is the fuel. That wanting is the fire. Now bring attention to the quality of holding. Can you release it? Not the sensation — you do not need to push the sensation away — but the wanting of it. Notice what happens when the wanting stops: the sensation may remain, but the burning subsides. This cooling — even for a moment — is a taste of Nibbāna. The leper's relief was illusory; this cooling is real.

### Seeing the Pit of Embers

Recall a sense pleasure you once pursued eagerly — a meal, a purchase, a relationship. Feel the memory of the wanting that drove you. Now ask: was that wanting actually satisfied, or was it merely temporarily soothed? See the pit of embers for what it is: a fire that gives the illusion of relief while the disease of craving remains unhealed.

**The logic this reveals:** Suffering is not in the sensation but in the fire of craving that feeds on it. Remove the fuel, and the fire ceases. The Arahant does not "go" anywhere — he simply no longer burns.
