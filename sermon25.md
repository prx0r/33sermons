# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 25

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Fire Sermon Deepened

The Ādittapariyāya — the Fire Sermon — is one of the most famous discourses of the Buddha. "All is burning," He declared to the thousand bhikkhus at Gayāsīsa. The eye is burning, forms are burning, eye-consciousness is burning, eye-contact is burning, whatever feeling arises with that contact is burning — burning with the fire of lust, the fire of hate, the fire of delusion, burning with birth, decay, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair.

But this burning is not arbitrary. Fire burns because it has fuel. Remove the fuel, and the fire goes out. So too with the fires of the senses: they burn because we grasp.

### The Fire That Goes Nowhere

When a fire goes out because its fuel is consumed, where does it go? This question has haunted philosophers. But the Buddha turns it around: the question assumes that the fire was a thing, a substance, an entity. But fire is a process — a phenomenon of burning dependent on fuel. When the fuel is removed, the process ceases. It does not "go" anywhere, because it was never a "thing" that could travel.

So too with the Arahant, who is like a fire that has gone out for lack of fuel. The five aggregates still function during life — the body experiences, the mind cognises — but there is no fuel of craving to sustain the fire of becoming. At death, the process ceases utterly. Not annihilation — for annihilation would require a self to be annihilated — but cessation of the burning.

### Rāga, Dosa, Moha

The three fires are *rāga* (lust), *dosa* (hate), and *moha* (delusion). They burn because they feed on objects. The eye lusts after forms, the ear after sounds, the mind after ideas. But when the mind sees the danger in these fires, it withdraws the fuel of enjoyment (*assāda*), sees the danger (*ādīnava*), and finds freedom (*nissaraṇa*).

Nibbāna is literally the extinguishing of these fires. When the fires are out, what remains? Not a thing — but not nothing either. What remains is the cool, the stillness, the peace that words cannot adequately convey.

## Plain-English Notes

### āditta (Burning)
Everything in the sensory realm is ablaze with the fires of defilement. The sense bases and their objects are not the problem — the fire of craving is.

### upādāna (Fuel/Grasping)
The same word means both "fuel" and "clinging." The insight is profound: grasping is the fuel that keeps the fire of suffering burning.

### nibbāna (Extinction)
Literally "going out," as a fire goes out. Not annihilation, but the cessation of the burning of defilement.

### rāga, dosa, moha
Lust, hate, and delusion — the three root defilements. All other defilements stem from these. Their complete eradication is Nibbāna.

## Meditations

### Feeling the Fire Go Out

Sit with a pleasant sensation — perhaps the warmth of the sun or a comfortable posture. Notice the subtle wanting, the desire for the pleasant feeling to continue. That wanting is the fuel. Now bring attention to the quality of holding. Can you release it? Not the sensation, but the wanting of it. Notice what happens when the wanting stops: the sensation may remain, but the burning subsides. This cooling — even for a moment — is a taste of Nibbāna.

**The logic this reveals:** Suffering is not in the sensation but in the fire of craving that feeds on it. Remove the fuel, and the fire ceases.
