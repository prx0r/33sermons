# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 04

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*

---

## The Teaching

### The Circuit of This-ness and Otherwise-ness

Towards the end of the last sermon, we were trying to explain how the process of the saṃsāric journey of beings could be understood even with the couple of terms itthabhāva and aññathābhāva, or this-ness and otherwise-ness. The verse from the Sutta Nipāta says:

> *Taṇhā dutiyo puriso, dīghamaddhāna saṃsāraṃ, itthabhāvaññathābhāvaṃ, saṃsāraṃ nātivattati.*
>
> "The man with craving as his companion, faring on for a long time in saṃsāra, does not transcend the round, which is of the nature of a this-ness and an otherwise-ness."

This is further proof that the two terms imply a circuit. It is a circuit between a 'here' and a 'there', or a 'this-ness' and an 'otherwise-ness'. It is a turning round, an alternation or a circuitous journey. It is like a rotation on the spot. It is an ambivalence between a here and a there.

It should be clearly understood that the passage of consciousness from here to a mother's womb is not a movement from one place to another, as in the case of the body. In reality, it is only a difference of point of view, and not a transmigration of a soul. In other words, when consciousness leaves this body and comes to stay in a mother's womb, when it is fully established there, 'that' place becomes a 'this' place. From the point of view of that consciousness, the 'there' becomes a 'here'. Consequently, from the new point of view, what was earlier a 'here', becomes a 'there'. What was formerly 'that place' has now become 'this place' and vice versa. That way, what actually is involved here, is a change of point of view. So it does not mean completely leaving one place and going to another, as is usually meant by the journey of an individual.

### The Seed and the Field

In the Aṅguttara Nikāya one comes across the following statement by the Buddha: *Kammaṃ khettaṃ, viññāṇaṃ bījaṃ, taṇhā sineho.* "Kamma is the field, consciousness is the seed, craving is the moisture." This, in effect, means that consciousness grows in the field of kamma with craving as the moisture.

When consciousness gets established in a mother's womb, with the condition of latency in the least, name-and-form begins to grow. It grows, as it were, with a flush of branches, in the form of the six sense bases, to produce a fresh tree of suffering. It is this idea that is voiced by the following well known verse in the Dhammapada:

> *Yathāpi mūle anupaddave daḷhe, chinno pi rukkho punareva rūhati, evam pi taṇhānusaye anūhate, nibbattati dukkham idaṃ punappunaṃ.*
>
> "Just as a tree, so long as its root is unharmed and firm, though once cut down, will none the less grow up again, even so, when craving's latency is not yet rooted out, this suffering gets reborn again and again."

### Selā Therī's Answer to Māra

Māra put a riddle to the Arahant nun Selā:

> *Ken'idaṃ pakataṃ bimbaṃ, ko nu bimbassa kārako, kvannu bimbaṃ samuppannaṃ, kvannu bimbaṃ nirujjhati?*
>
> "By whom was this image wrought, who is the maker of this image, where has this image arisen, and where does the image cease?"

The image meant here is one's body, or one's outward appearance which, for the conventional world, is name-and-form. Selā Therī gives her answer:

> *Nayidaṃ attakataṃ bimbaṃ, nayidaṃ parakataṃ aghaṃ, hetuṃ paṭicca sambhūtaṃ, hetubhaṅgā nirujjhati. Yathā aññataraṃ bījaṃ, khette vuttaṃ virūhati, pathavīrasañcāgamma, sinehañca tadūbhayaṃ. Evaṃ khandhā ca dhātuyo, cha ca āyatanā ime, hetuṃ paṭicca sambhūtā, hetubhaṅgā nirujjhare.*
>
> "Neither self-wrought is this image, nor yet other-wrought is this misery, by reason of a cause, it came to be, by breaking up the cause, it ceases to be. Just as in the case of a certain seed, which when sown on the field would feed on the taste of the earth and moisture, and by these two would grow. Even so, all these aggregates and elements and the six sense-bases, by reason of a cause, have come to be; by breaking up the cause, they cease to be."

The Arahant's insight: the "image" (name-and-form, the body, the person) is not self-made, not other-made, but arises dependently. When the cause breaks, it ceases. There is no "who" — only a "what" that arises and ceases by conditions.

### Nissaya: The Tendency to Lean On

In the sutta terminology the word nissaya is often used to denote the tendency to get attached to worldly concepts. The cognate word nissita is also used alongside. It goes deeper than common usage, to convey the idea of 'leaning on' something. Worldlings have a tendency to tenaciously grasp the concepts in worldly usage, to cling to them dogmatically and lean on them. They believe that the words they use have a reality of their own, that they are categorically true in their own right. Their attitude towards concepts is tinctured by craving, conceit and views.

We come across this word nissita in quite a number of important suttas. In the Channovādasutta of the Majjhima Nikāya there is a cryptic passage:

> *Nissitassa calitaṃ, anissitassa calitaṃ natthi. Calite asati passaddhi, passaddhiyā sati nati na hoti, natiyā asati āgatigati na hoti, āgatigatiyā asati cutūpapāto na hoti, cutūpapāte asati nev'idha na huraṃ na ubhayamantare. Es' ev' anto dukhassa.*
>
> "To the one attached, there is wavering. To the unattached one, there is no wavering. When there is no wavering, there is calm. When there is calm, there is no inclination. When there is no inclination, there is no coming and going. When there is no coming and going, there is no death and birth. When there is no death and birth, there is neither a 'here' nor a 'there' nor a 'between the two'. This itself is the end of suffering."

To the one who leans on something, there is wavering or movement. He is perturbable. The one released is not. When there is no wavering, there is calm. When there is calm, there is no inclination (nati — bending). When there is no bending, there is no coming and going. When there is no coming and going, there is no death and birth. And when there is no death and birth, there is neither a 'here' nor a 'there' nor a 'between the two'.

Notice that nati, "bending," is the same word the commentaries used to explain nāma as "bending towards an object." The unattached one has no bending at all. He does not lean in any direction.

### Kaccāyanagottasutta: Right View and the Middle Path

There is a discourse in the Nidāna section of the Saṃyutta Nikāya, which affords us a deeper insight into the meaning of the word nissaya. It is the Kaccāyanagottasutta, also significant for its deeper analysis of right view. The Buddha introduces it thus:

> *Dvayanissito khvāyaṃ, Kaccāyana, loko yebhuyyena: atthitañceva natthitañca.*
>
> "This world, Kaccāyana, for the most part, bases its views on two things: on existence and non-existence."

The Buddha first points out that the worldlings mostly base themselves on a duality, the two conflicting views of existence and non-existence, or 'is' and 'is not'. They would either hold on to the dogmatic view of eternalism, or would cling to nihilism. Now as to the right view of the noble disciple, it takes into account the process of arising as well as the process of cessation, and thereby avoids both extremes. This is the insight that illuminates the middle path.

Then the Buddha goes on to give a more detailed explanation:

> *Upayupādānābhinivesavinibandho khvāyaṃ, Kaccāyana, loko yebhuyyena. Tañcāyaṃ upayupādānaṃ cetaso adhiṭṭhānaṃ abhinivesānusayaṃ na upeti na upādiyati nādhiṭṭhāti: 'attā me'ti. 'Dukkham eva uppajjamānaṃ uppajjati, dukkhaṃ nirujjhamānaṃ nirujjhatī'ti na kaṅkhati na vicikicchati aparapaccayā ñāṇam ev' assa ettha hoti. Ettāvatā kho, Kaccāyana, sammā diṭṭhi hoti.*
>
> "The world, Kaccāyana, for the most part, is given to approaching, grasping, entering into and getting entangled as regards views. Whoever does not approach, grasp, and take his stand upon that proclivity towards approaching and grasping, that mental standpoint, namely the idea: 'This is my soul', he knows that what arises is just suffering and what ceases is just suffering. Thus, he is not in doubt, is not perplexed, and herein he has the knowledge that is not dependent on another. Thus far, Kaccāyana, he has right view."

The passage starts with a string of terms which has a deep philosophical significance. *Upaya* means 'approaching', *upādāna* is 'grasping', *abhinivesa* is 'entering into', and *vinibandha* is the consequent entanglement. The implication is that the worldling is prone to approach every object of experience with a tendency to grasp it as his own and to get entangled in it. The noble disciple, by understanding the law of dependent arising, does not approach, does not grasp, does not take a stand on any such proclivity. He sees that what arises is just suffering and what ceases is just suffering. He is not dependent on another for this knowledge. This is right view.

The Buddha then states the law of dependent arising itself as the middle path:

> *...Thus from ignorance as condition, preparations come to be; from preparations, consciousness; from consciousness, name-and-form; ... from becoming, birth; from birth, decay-and-death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief and despair come to be. Such is the arising of this entire mass of suffering.*
>
> *But with the complete fading away and cessation of ignorance comes the cessation of preparations... from the cessation of birth, comes the cessation of decay-and-death... Such is the cessation of this entire mass of suffering.*

It is clear from this declaration that in this context the law of dependent arising itself is called the middle path. Some prefer to call this the Buddha's metaphysical middle path, as it avoids both extremes of 'is' and 'is not'. The philosophical implications lead to the conclusion that the law of dependent arising enshrines a certain pragmatic principle, which dissolves the antinomian conflict in the world.

### The Conjoined Pair: You Cannot Separate the Rice-Cakes

The basic principle involved in dependent arising is idappaccayatā — the relatedness of this to that. As already illustrated by the two links birth and decay-and-death: when birth is there, decay-and-death come to be. The fact that this relatedness itself is the eternal law is clearly revealed by the Buddha:

> *Avijjāpaccayā, bhikkhave, saṅkhārā. Ya tatra tathatā avitathatā anaññathatā idappaccayatā, ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, paṭiccasamuppādo.*
>
> "From ignorance as condition, preparations come to be. That suchness therein, the invariability, the not-otherwiseness, the relatedness of this to that — this, monks, is called dependent arising."

Generally, the worldlings are engaged in an attempt to separate the two in this conjoined pair. Before the Buddha came into the scene, all religious teachers were trying to hold on to birth, while rejecting decay-and-death. But it was a vain struggle.

There is a story about the millionaire Kosiya, an extreme miser, who once developed a strong desire to eat rice-cakes. As he did not wish to share them with anyone else, he climbed up to the topmost storey of his mansion with his wife and got her to cook rice-cakes for him. To teach him a lesson, Venerable Mahā Moggallāna, who excelled in psychic powers, went through the air and appeared at the window as if on his alms round. Kosiya, wishing to dismiss this intruder with a tiny rice-cake, asked his wife to put a little bit of cake dough into the pan. She did so, but it became a big rice-cake through the venerable thera's psychic power. Further attempts to make tinier rice-cakes ended up in producing ever bigger and bigger ones. In the end, Kosiya thought of dismissing the monk with just one cake, but to his utter dismay, all the cakes got joined to each other to form a string of cakes. The couple then started pulling this string of cakes in either direction with all their might, to separate just one from it. But without success. At last they decided to let go and give up, and offered the entire string of cakes to the venerable Thera.

The Buddha's solution to the problem of existence is a similar let go-ism and giving up. It is a case of giving up all assets — *sabbūpadhipaṭinissagga*. You cannot separate these links from one another. Birth and decay-and-death are intertwined. This is a conjoined pair. So the solution here, is to let go. All those problems are due to taking up a standpoint. Therefore the kind of view sanctioned in this case, is one that leads to detachment and dispassion, one that goes against the tendency to grasp and hold on. It is by grasping and holding on that one comes into conflict with Māra. Māra's forte lies in seizing and grabbing. When Māra comes round to grab, if we can find some means of foiling his attempt, or make it impossible for him to grab, then Māra will have to accept defeat.

### The Satipaṭṭhānasutta: Internal and External Are Neighbors

There is a certain thematic paragraph which occurs at the end of each subsection in the Satipaṭṭhānasutta:

> *Iti ajjhattaṃ vā kāye kāyānupassī viharati, bahiddhā vā kāye kāyānupassī viharati, ajjhattabahiddhā vā kāye kāyānupassī viharati; samudayadhammānupassī vā kāyasmiṃ viharati, vayadhammānupassī vā kāyasmiṃ viharati, samudayavayadhammānupassī vā kāyasmiṃ viharati; 'atthi kāyo'ti vā pan'assa sati paccupaṭṭhitā hoti, yāvadeva ñāṇamattāya paṭissatimattāya; anissito ca viharati, na ca kiñci loke upādiyati.*
>
> "In this way he abides contemplating the body as a body internally, or he abides contemplating the body as a body externally, or he abides contemplating the body as a body both internally and externally. He abides contemplating the nature of arising in the body, or the nature of passing away in the body, or the nature of both arising and passing away in the body. Or else the mindfulness that 'there is a body' is established in him only to the extent necessary for knowledge and remembrance. And he abides independent, not clinging to anything in the world."

The word used is *anissita* — independent, not leaning towards anything. He does not cling to anything in the world. The word nissaya says something more than grasping. It means 'leaning on' or 'associating'.

This particular thematic paragraph is of paramount importance for insight meditation. Here, too, there is the mention of internal, ajjhatta, and external, bahiddhā. When one directs one's attention to one's own body and another's body separately, one might sometimes take these two concepts too seriously with a dogmatic attitude. One might think that there is actually something that could be called one's own or another's. But then the mode of attention next mentioned unifies the two, as internal-external, *ajjhattabahiddhā*, and presents them like a conjoined pair.

The farthest limit of the internal is the nearest limit of the external. The farthest limit of the external is the nearest limit of the internal. More strictly rendered, ajjhatta means inward and bahiddhā means outward. So here we have the duality of an inside and an outside.

Whatever portion one demarcates as one's own, just adjoining it and at its very gate is bahiddhā. And from the point of view of bahiddhā, its farthest limit and at its periphery is ajjhatta. This is a conjoined pair. These two are interrelated. So the implication is that these two are not opposed to each other. That is why, by attending to them both together, as ajjhattabahiddhā, that dogmatic involvement with a view is abandoned. This is what fosters the attitude of anissita — unattached.

### The Six Sense-Bases: Where the World Arises and Ceases

In the Saṃyutta Nikāya there is a verse which presents this idea in a striking manner:

> *Chasu loko samuppanno, chasu kubbati santhavaṃ, channam eva upādāya, chasu loko vihaññati.*
>
> "In the six the world arose, in the six it holds concourse, on the six themselves depending, in the six it has its woes."

The verse seems to say that the world has arisen in the six, that it has associations in the six, and that depending on those very six, the world comes to grief.

Though the commentators advance an interpretation of this six, according to which the first line refers to the six internal sense bases and the second to the six external sense bases, these two — internal and external — are in fact a conjoined pair. The world arises at their meeting point. The entire teaching of dependent arising is visible at the six sense-doors, here and now. The penetrative wisdom that sees the rise and fall — *udayatthagāminī paññā* — sees through the rigid encrustation of the two dogmatic views of existence and non-existence. When one sees the arising aspect of the world through one's own six sense bases, one finds it impossible to hold the view that nothing exists. Similarly, when one sees the cessation, one sees no possibility to hold the extreme view that everything exists.

### The Dhamma as Raft and Scaffolding

What is found in the Satipaṭṭhānasutta is a group of concepts. These concepts serve only as a scaffolding for the systematic development of mindfulness and knowledge. The Buddha often compared his Dhamma to a raft: *nittharaṇatthāya no gahaṇatthāya* — "for crossing over and not for holding on to." Accordingly, what we have here are so many scaffoldings for the up-building of mindfulness and knowledge.

Probably due to the lack of understanding of this deep philosophy enshrined in the Satipaṭṭhānasutta, many sects of Buddhism took up these concepts in a spirit of dogmatic adherence. That dogmatic attitude of clinging on is like the attempt to cling on to the scaffoldings and to live on in them.

## Plain-English Notes

### Nissaya / Nissita (Leaning On / Association)

This is the key term of this sermon. Nissaya is not just "clinging" — it's the more subtle tendency to *lean on* concepts as if they have a reality of their own. When you believe the word "table" corresponds to a real, independent table, you're leaning on the concept. When you think "I am a person" as if that's a fixed truth, you're leaning on the concept.

The unattached one (anissita) doesn't lean. When you don't lean, you don't waver. When you don't waver, you're calm. When you're calm, there's no bending (nati). When there's no bending, there's no coming and going. The entire chain of suffering unravels from the simple act of not leaning.

### The Conjoined Pair (Birth and Death, Internal and External)

You cannot have birth without death. They're not two separate things — they're a pair. Like the rice-cakes that Kosiya couldn't separate: if you try to pull them apart, the string just gets longer. The only solution is to let go of both.

This applies to all pairs: existence/non-existence, self/other, inside/outside, arising/ceasing. The right view is not to choose one or the other, but to see the relatedness itself — idappaccayatā — and let go of the entire paired structure.

### Internal and External Are Neighbors

The farthest limit of the internal is the nearest limit of the external. This is a profound insight. The boundary between "me" and "not-me" is not a wall — it's a meeting point. Realizing this dissolves the dogmatic attitude toward self and other. The meditator contemplates both together (ajjhattabahiddhā) and thereby abandons the view that takes either side as absolute.

### The Dhamma as Raft

The teachings are tools, not destinations. The Satipaṭṭhāna is scaffolding — you build mindfulness and knowledge on it, but you don't live in the scaffolding. The concepts (body, feeling, mind, mind-objects) are means to see rise and fall. Once you see, the concepts are relinquished.

## Meditations

### 1. The Leaning Meditation (Nissaya)

Sit and observe your relationship to your thoughts. For each thought that arises, notice: are you leaning on it? Do you believe it's true? Do you take it as "my thought"?

Now try something different: let the thought arise, but don't lean toward it or away from it. Don't grab it, don't push it. Let it be there without your weight on it. Notice how the thought, when not leaned on, has no power to move you.

Practice this for 10 minutes with whatever arises: sensations, emotions, thoughts. The posture is: "I am not leaning on this. This is not my support."

**The logic this reveals:** The Channovādasutta says: to the one attached (nissita), there is wavering (calita). The entire saṃsāric journey — coming and going, death and birth — is driven by this leaning. When the leaning stops, the wavering stops. The calm that follows is not a state you create — it's what's left when you stop leaning.

---

### 2. The Neighbors Meditation (Internal and External)

Pick a boundary: the edge of your skin, the threshold of your room, the distinction between "my thoughts" and "the world."

Now ask: what is the farthest limit of "inside"? Is it your skin? Your thoughts? Your sense of self? And what is the nearest limit of "outside"? Is it the air touching your skin? The sounds entering your ears?

Notice: the farthest inside and the nearest outside are the same point. They meet. They're not two separate territories with a wall between them — they're neighbors sharing a border.

Practice: sit with your eyes closed. Feel your breath at the nostrils. Is that "inside" or "outside"? The air enters — outside becomes inside. The air exits — inside becomes outside. At the point of contact, there is no inside and no outside — just the meeting.

**The logic this reveals:** The Satipaṭṭhāna method has you contemplate internally, externally, and both together. The "both together" is not a synthesis of two opposites — it's a seeing that they were never separate. The dogmatic view of "self" and "world" collapses when you see they define each other at the same meeting point.

---

### 3. The Kosiya Meditation (Letting Go of the String)

Think of a problem you're trying to solve by separating two things you don't want linked. You want pleasure without pain. You want life without death. You want approval without criticism.

Now see the string of rice-cakes. Every attempt to pull one cake free makes the string longer. The more you try to separate the pair, the more entangled you become.

Practice: instead of pulling, let go. Don't try to separate pleasure from pain, life from death, self from other. See them as a conjoined pair. The letting go is *sabbūpadhipaṭinissagga* — the relinquishment of all assets. You give up not just the bad side of the pair, but also the good side. Both.

**The logic this reveals:** Māra's power is in grabbing. When you grab nothing, Māra has nothing to grab. The victory is not in winning the tug-of-war — it's in dropping the rope entirely.

---

### 4. The Seed Meditation (Watching What Grows)

The Buddha said: kamma is the field, consciousness is the seed, craving is the moisture.

Observe your mind as a field. Each thought is a seed. Each desire is moisture. What's growing?

Now notice the latency (anusaya) — the *tendency* for seeds to sprout. Even when you're not actively thinking or intending, there's a residual moistness — the latency. This is enough for the seed to grow.

The third instance of the Cetanāsutta (from sermon 3) is relevant here: no intention, no construction, *and* no latency. Only then does nothing grow.

**Practice:** Sit and notice the background hum of "I am." That's the latency. See it without feeding it. Don't add moisture (craving). Don't pull the seed (intention). Just let the background hum be there without your participation. Watch to see if it can sustain itself without being watered.

**The logic this reveals:** Selā Therī said the image (bimba) is neither self-made nor other-made — it arises by cause and ceases by the breaking of the cause. The cause is the triangle of field + seed + moisture. When any one is removed, the image ceases. You don't need to destroy the image — you just stop watering the seed.

---

## Summary of the Logic

Sermon 4 shows that the entire saṃsāric structure rests on a subtle leaning (nissaya):

1. **Consciousness doesn't travel** — it changes point of view. The "there" becomes "here." Saṃsāra is not a journey through space but a rotation on the spot between this-ness and otherwise-ness.

2. **Nissaya (leaning on)** is the engine of saṃsāra. You lean on concepts, on "I am," on the idea that words have fixed realities. The unattached one (anissita) doesn't lean and therefore doesn't waver.

3. **Right view** (Kaccāyanagottasutta) is not a position — it's the avoidance of all positions. It sees arising and ceasing without fixing on "exists" or "does not exist." The middle path is not a compromise between extremes; it's the seeing that the extremes are a conjoined pair.

4. **The conjoined pair** (birth/death, inside/outside, existence/non-existence) cannot be separated by pulling. The only solution is to let go — *sabbūpadhipaṭinissagga*.

5. **The Satipaṭṭhāna** is scaffolding. The concepts of body, feeling, mind, and mind-objects are tools for seeing rise and fall, not objects of attachment. The meditator abides anissita — not leaning on anything.
