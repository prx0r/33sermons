# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 33

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### Returning to the Beginning

We began this series of thirty-three sermons with a single verse. We close with that same verse. It has been our compass, our kammaṭṭhāna, our meditation subject throughout. Now, having traversed the entire path from the first spark of insight to the full extinguishment, let us revisit each phrase with the understanding gained along the way.

### Etaṃ Santaṃ — "This is Peaceful"

From the first sermon, we established that Nibbāna is not a place, a state, or a metaphysical entity. It is the peace that comes when the mind ceases its preparations. In sermon after sermon, we saw how the mind constructs its world through saṅkhāra — through the activity of preparing, forming, concocting. This peace is not the peace of a dead stone but the peace of a mind that has seen through its own activity of building.

We explored how "santaṃ" relates to "santi" — the stilling of passion. We saw in sermon 3 how the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion are themselves the disturbance. When those fires are extinguished, what remains is not a "thing" but a peace that is not conditioned by any thing.

### Etaṃ Paṇītaṃ — "This is Excellent"

The excellence of Nibbāna is not comparative — it is not excellent because it is better than something else. It is excellent in the sense of being supreme, unsurpassed. In sermon 12, we examined how even the highest meditative attainments — the formless spheres — are conditioned and therefore not excellent in the ultimate sense. Nibbāna alone is paṇīta because it is unconditioned, asaṅkhata.

But we must be careful. Even this statement can be misunderstood. Nibbāna is not a "thing" that is unconditioned while everything else is conditioned. The unconditioned is the cessation of the activity of conditioning. It is like the silence that is left when all sounds cease — the silence is not a "thing" but the absence of the activity of sounding.

### Sabbasaṅkhārasamatho — "The Stilling of All Preparations"

This phrase has been at the heart of our investigation. Saṅkhāra has many meanings — formations, preparations, conditioned things, volitional activities. In sermon 5, we saw how saṅkhāra is the activity of the mind that constructs and prepares. In sermon 13, we examined the saṅkhāra link in dependent arising — how volitional formations condition consciousness.

The stilling of all preparations is not a violent suppression of the mind's natural activity. It is the natural cessation that occurs when the mind sees through its own tendency to prepare. It is like asking a busy person to sit down — there is no struggle; the activity simply stops when the purpose is seen.

### Sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo — "The Relinquishment of All Assets"

Upadhi — assets, substrata, grounds of attachment — was the subject of sermon 10 and many subsequent teachings. These are the "things" we cling to as mine: possessions, relationships, body, mind, views, even life itself. Each sermon in this series has been an invitation to examine one more layer of what we hold dear.

The relinquishment of all assets is not an act of throwing things away. It is an inner letting go — the realization that nothing can be owned. In sermon 22, we saw that even the idea of "being" an Arahant is an upadhi to be relinquished. The Arahant does not think "I am liberated." The liberation is not owned; it simply is.

### Taṇhakkhayo — "The Destruction of Craving"

Craving — taṇhā — is the origin of suffering, as the Buddha declared in the first sermon at Sārnāth. In sermon 2, we explored how craving is the cause of dukkha, and in sermon 6, we saw how it proliferates through papañca — conceptual proliferation. Taṇhā is not just obvious desire; it is the subtle thirst that drives all becoming.

The destruction of craving is not the annihilation of desire through force. It is the natural end of thirst when one has drunk from the spring of seeing. In sermon 18, we examined how the Arahant still feels pleasant and painful sensations but does not crave or aversion them. The destruction of craving is the absence of reactivity.

### Virāgo — "Detachment"

Virāga is often translated as "dispassion" or "detachment." But it is not cold indifference. It is the fading away of lust, the cooling of passion. In sermon 9, we explored how virāga is the natural result of seeing the true nature of phenomena — when you see a mirage for what it is, the thirst for it naturally fades.

This is perhaps the most practical term in the whole verse. The path to Nibbāna is the gradual cultivation of virāga — not by suppressing desire, but by understanding it. Each sermon has offered tools for this understanding: the contemplation of impermanence (sermon 7), the contemplation of not-self (sermon 8), the contemplation of dependent arising (sermons 11-16).

### Nirodho — "Cessation"

Nirodha is the cessation of suffering and its causes. In sermon 20, we examined the noble truth of cessation: "yo tassāyeva taṇhāya asesavirāganirodho cāgo paṭinissaggo mutti anālayo" — the complete fading away and cessation of that craving, the abandoning, relinquishing, release, non-dependence.

Cessation is not annihilation because there is nothing substantial to annihilate. What ceases is the illusion of self, the activity of clinging, the process of becoming. In sermon 25, we saw how even consciousness, when it ceases to land on any object, is not "destroyed" but "becomes unestablished." The cessation of saṃsāra is like the cessation of a dream — when you wake up, the dream does not go anywhere; it simply ceases because its conditions are no longer present.

### Nibbānaṃ — "Extinction"

And so we arrive at the word itself: Nibbāna. The going out of the flame. The extinguishing of the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion. In sermon 1, we established that Nibbāna is not a place but a quality, not a destination but a realization. In sermon 4, we examined the fire simile of the Ādittapariyāyasutta — all is burning. Nibbāna is the coolness when the burning ceases.

Throughout this series, we have seen that Nibbāna is not an "it" that can be described. It is not a positive reality nor a negative void. It is the end of the problem of grasping. When the Buddha was asked about the fate of the Tathāgata after death, he dismissed the question as improperly framed. The fire that has gone out — where does it go? The question itself is the problem.

### The Holy Life Merged in Nibbāna

The Buddha declared that the holy life is lived "saṃsīdati" — it merges, it sinks — in Nibbāna. Like a river that flows into the ocean, the holy life flows into Nibbāna. The path and the goal are not separate. The walking of the path is itself the realization of the goal.

In sermon 14, we examined how the noble eightfold path is not a means to an end separate from itself. Right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration — when these are developed, Nibbāna is realized. Not as a result, but as the perfection of the path itself.

### The Mind Stilled

We titled this series "Nibbāna — The Mind Stilled." This is the essence of the entire teaching. The mind, when it sees things as they are, naturally becomes still. Not forced into stillness, not hypnotized into stillness, not drugged into stillness — but stilled by the power of seeing.

The stilled mind is not a dead mind. It is a mind that is fully alive, fully awake, fully responsive — but without the tremor of craving. It is like the flame of a lamp in a windless place — steady, bright, unwavering.

The thirty-three sermons have covered many teachings: the five aggregates (sermon 17), dependent arising (sermons 11-16), the four establishments of mindfulness (sermon 21), the noble eightfold path (sermon 14), the four noble truths (sermons 19-20), consciousness and Nibbāna (sermon 28), the four types of grasping (sermon 29), the middle way (sermon 30), the destruction of the influxes (sermon 31), and the Arahant's freedom (sermon 32). But all these teachings point to one thing: the stilling of the mind.

### The Dhamma as a Raft

One final reflection. The Dhamma is a raft for crossing over, not for carrying on one's head. All thirty-three sermons are themselves saṅkhāras — preparations, constructs, formations. They are useful only insofar as they help you cross the flood. When you have crossed, you let go of the raft.

Do not grasp at the words. Do not grasp at the teachings. Do not grasp at Nibbāna. Let go of everything. That letting go — that complete relinquishment — is itself Nibbāna.

> *Āsavānaṃ khayā anāsavaṃ cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ diṭṭheva dhamme sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā upasampajja viharati.*
> "With the destruction of the influxes, he dwells having realized here and now the influx-free liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom."

May all beings cross the flood. May all beings realize the stilling of the mind.

## Plain-English Notes

### Summary Glossary

Throughout these thirty-three sermons, we have encountered the following key terms. Their convergence is the teaching itself:

**Nibbāna** — Extinction, the going out of the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion. Not annihilation, but the cessation of craving.

**Saṅkhāra** — Preparations, formations, volitional activities. The mind's activity of constructing experience.

**Upadhi** — Assets, substrata, the things we cling to as supports for identity.

**Taṇhā** — Craving, thirst, the cause of suffering.

**Virāga** — Detachment, dispassion, the fading away of lust.

**Nirodha** — Cessation, the stopping of the process of clinging.

**Viññāṇa** — Consciousness, which arises depending on conditions.

**Anidassana** — Non-manifestative, consciousness without an object.

**Appatiṭṭhita** — Unestablished, consciousness without standing.

**Upādāna** — Grasping, fuel. The four types: sense-desires, views, rites-and-rituals, self-doctrine.

**Āsava** — Influxes, cankers. The three: sensuality, existence, ignorance.

**Sītibhūta** — Become cool, the Arahant's state.

**Tādi** — The such-like one, the Arahant unshaken by worldly conditions.

**Dukkha** — Suffering, unsatisfactoriness, the burden of the aggregates.

**Anattā** — Not-self, the absence of a permanent essence in all phenomena.

**Anicca** — Impermanence, the transitory nature of all conditioned things.

**Paṭicca Samuppāda** — Dependent arising, the middle way itself.

## Meditations

### The Final Letting Go — A Guided Reflection

Sit quietly. Bring to mind all that you have learned — every sermon, every teaching, every insight. Now let it all go. Do not hold even the most profound realization. Simply sit in the stillness that remains when all grasping has ceased.

If a thought arises, let it pass. If an insight arises, let it pass. If a sense of peace arises, let it pass. Do not hold anything. Be like a room with all the doors and windows open — the wind passes through freely, leaving no trace.

### The Thirty-Three Reflections

For each of the thirty-three sermons, recall the central teaching. Not as a memory exercise, but as a way of seeing how all teachings converge:

1. The theme verse itself — this peace is the stilling.
2. Craving is the origin — see it, and it ceases.
3. The fires burn from fuel — remove the fuel.
4. All is burning — see the burning everywhere.
5. Preparations construct the world — see the construction.
6. Proliferation entangles — see the entanglement.
7. Impermanence — nothing lasts.
8. Not-self — nothing owns.
9. Detachment — the natural result of seeing.
10. Assets relinquished — nothing to hold.
11-16. Dependent arising — the middle way.
17. The aggregates — the burden.
18. Feeling without craving — possible.
19-20. The four truths — the map.
21. Mindfulness — the direct path.
22-27. The gradual training — the unfolding.
28. Consciousness unestablished — freedom.
29. Four graspings as fuel — all burning.
30. Middle way — beyond existence and non-existence.
31. Influxes destroyed — the root cut.
32. The Arahant's freedom — cool and released.
33. The mind stilled — here and now.

### The Meditation on the Theme Verse

Take the theme verse as your final meditation subject. Recite it slowly, phrase by phrase. Let each phrase sink into the mind:

*Etaṃ santaṃ* — This is peaceful. Rest in that peace.

*Etaṃ paṇītaṃ* — This is excellent. Rest in that excellence.

*Sabbasaṅkhārasamatho* — The stilling of all preparations. Let all preparations cease.

*Sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo* — The relinquishment of all assets. Let all holdings go.

*Taṇhakkhayo* — The destruction of craving. Let all thirst end.

*Virāgo* — Detachment. Let all passion fade.

*Nirodho* — Cessation. Let all clinging stop.

*Nibbānaṃ* — Extinction. Let the fires go out.

**The logic this reveals:** The entire Dhamma is contained in this single verse. When the mind is stilled, Nibbāna is realized. The path is the goal. The seeking is the finding. The journey is the arrival.
