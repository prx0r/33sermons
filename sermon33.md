# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 33

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### Returning to the Theme Verse

We began this series of thirty-three sermons with a single verse. We close with that same verse. It has been our compass throughout. Now, having traversed the entire path from the first spark of insight to the full extinguishment, we revisit each phrase with the understanding gained along the way — and we see that the first sermon already contained the last.

### Nīla Parting the Sea with an Iron Bar

The path-knowledge is like the story of Nīla, who struck the sea with an iron bar. With one blow, the waters parted, and he saw the very bottom. Even the first glimpse — stream-entry — reveals the unconditioned. The path does not build Nibbāna gradually; it uncovers what was always there but hidden by the waters of craving.

When the iron bar of wisdom strikes the sea of preparations, the waters part. One sees the bottom. One sees Nibbāna. Not as a distant goal, not as a future reward, but as the ground that was always present beneath the waves of becoming. The first glimpse is already the truth. The remaining path is the deepening and stabilizing of that vision.

### Etaṃ Santaṃ — This Is Peaceful

From sermon 1, we established that Nibbāna is not a place, a state, or a metaphysical entity. It is the peace that comes when the mind ceases its preparations. In sermon 3, we saw how the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion are themselves the disturbance. In sermon 5, we explored saṅkhāra as the mind's activity of constructing experience. When those fires are extinguished and that constructing ceases, what remains is not a "thing" but a peace that is not conditioned by any thing.

### Etaṃ Paṇītaṃ — This Is Excellent

The excellence of Nibbāna is not comparative. It is excellent because it is unsurpassed — the unconditioned, asaṅkhata. In sermon 12, we examined how even the highest formless attainments are conditioned. In sermon 27, we explored the thirty-three synonyms — each a pointer, none the thing itself. Nibbāna alone is paṇīta, not as a thing among things, but as the cessation of all things.

### Sabbasaṅkhārasamatho — The Stilling of All Preparations

Saṅkhāra — the activity of the mind that constructs and prepares. In sermon 13, we examined the saṅkhāra link in dependent arising. In sermons 5 and 14, we saw how the mind builds its world moment by moment through volitional activity. The stilling of all preparations is not a violent suppression. It is like asking a busy person to sit down — there is no struggle; the activity simply stops when its futility is seen.

### Sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo — The Relinquishment of All Assets

Upadhi — the assets, the substrata we cling to as mine. In sermon 10, we examined upadhi as the ground of attachment. In sermon 22, we saw that even the idea of being an Arahant is an upadhi to be relinquished. The Arahant does not think "I am liberated." The liberation is not owned; it simply is. All assets relinquished — even the asset of a self that could possess them.

### Taṇhakkhayo — The Destruction of Craving

Craving — taṇhā — is the origin of suffering, as the Buddha declared at Sārnāth. In sermon 2, we explored craving as the cause. In sermon 6, we saw how it proliferates through papañca — conceptual proliferation. In sermon 18, we examined how the Arahant still feels sensations but does not crave. The destruction of craving is not the annihilation of desire through force; it is the natural end of thirst when one has drunk from the spring of seeing.

### Virāgo — Detachment

Virāga — the fading away of lust. In sermon 9, we explored how virāga is the natural result of seeing the true nature of phenomena. When you see a mirage for what it is, the thirst for it naturally fades. The path to Nibbāna is the gradual cultivation of virāga — not by suppressing desire, but by understanding it.

### Nirodho — Cessation

Nirodha — the cessation of suffering and its causes. In sermon 20, we examined the third noble truth. Cessation is not annihilation because there is nothing substantial to annihilate. What ceases is the illusion of self, the activity of clinging, the process of becoming. In sermon 25, we saw how even consciousness, when it no longer lands on any object, is not destroyed but becomes unestablished. The cessation of saṃsāra is like waking from a dream — the dream does not go anywhere; it simply ceases because its conditions are no longer present.

### Nibbānaṃ — Extinction

And the word itself — Nibbāna. The going out of the flame. In sermon 1, we established that Nibbāna is not a place but a quality. In sermon 4, we examined the Ādittapariyāyasutta — all is burning. In sermon 23, we explored the fire simile in depth. Nibbāna is the coolness when the burning ceases.

### Rivers Merging in the Sea

The holy life merges in Nibbāna like rivers flow into the sea. The Buddha declared that the holy life is lived *saṃsīdati* — it merges, it sinks — in Nibbāna. The path and the goal are not separate. The walking of the path is itself the realization of the goal.

Each river keeps its name — the Ganges, the Yamunā, the Sarasvatī — as long as it flows. But when it reaches the sea, it loses its separate identity and becomes the sea itself. So too the path: right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration — as long as the path is being developed, it has a name. But when the goal is realized, the path and the goal are one.

### Examination Simile

As soon as you answer the paper correctly, you pass immediately. The certificate is there. You do not wait for the results to be announced; the certitude is immediate. This is *aññā* — immediate knowledge, immediate certitude.

The Arahant's knowledge is like this. When the paper of the four truths is answered correctly, when the three characteristics are seen directly, the certitude is immediate. There is no waiting. The Arahant knows: "Destroyed is birth, lived is the holy life, done is what had to be done, there is nothing further beyond this."

### Fire Going Out

The culmination of the fire simile that has run through the entire series. In sermon 1, we introduced the fire that goes out when the fuel is removed. In sermon 4, we saw all is burning. In sermon 23, we explored the fire at death. Now, in this final sermon, the fire is out. Not because it has been forcibly extinguished, but because the fuel of grasping has been consumed. The mind is stilled.

The fire that has gone out — where does it go? The question is wrongly framed. The fire does not go anywhere; it simply ceases because its conditions are no longer present. So too with the mind stilled: it does not go to Nibbāna as to a place. It is Nibbāna. The stilling itself is the goal.

### Tangle Disentangled

From the first sermon's Jaṭāsutta — "A tangle within, a tangle without" — we have been working to disentangle the knot. The Jaṭāsutta says:

> *Yattha nāmañca rūpañca, asesaṃ uparujjhati*
> "Where name and form cease without remainder."

Where name-and-form, resistance (*paṭigha*), and perception of form (*rūpasaññā*) are cut off, there the tangle is disentangled. The entire series has been a gradual disentangling — first of gross grasping, then of subtle views, then of the sense of self itself. The tangle is not something to be solved intellectually. It is to be seen through. When the mind sees the tangle as it is — as a dependently arisen process, not as a real knot — the tangle unravels of its own accord.

### Key Teachings Reviewed

From the thirty-three sermons, several key teachings converge: **nāma-rūpa** as the support of consciousness (sermons 5, 11, 17), **papañca** as conceptual proliferation that entangles the mind (sermon 6), **upādāna** as the four fuels that keep the fire burning (sermon 29), **paṭicca samuppāda** as the middle way itself (sermons 11-16, 30), **asmimāna** as the subtle conceit "I am" (sermon 24), **Bāhiya's** direct path of seeing without proliferating (sermon 26), the **two Nibbāna elements** — with and without residual fuel (sermon 23), the **three doors to liberation** — signless, desireless, void (sermon 25), and the **thirty-three synonyms** — each a finger pointing at the moon (sermon 27).

### The Mind Stilled

We titled this series "Nibbāna — The Mind Stilled." The stilled mind is not a dead mind; it is a mind fully alive, fully awake, fully responsive — but without the tremor of craving. Like the flame of a lamp in a windless place — steady, bright, unwavering. Not a mind forcibly stopped, but a mind that has come to rest because the fuel of grasping has been consumed. This is Nibbāna. Not a place, not a state, not a thing. The stilling itself.

### The Dhamma as a Raft

One final reflection. The Dhamma is a raft for crossing over, not for carrying on one's head. All thirty-three sermons are themselves saṅkhāras — preparations, constructs, formations. They are useful only insofar as they help you cross the flood. When you have crossed, you let go of the raft.

Do not grasp at the words. Do not grasp at the teachings. Do not grasp at Nibbāna. Let go of everything. That letting go — that complete relinquishment — is itself Nibbāna.

> *Āsavānaṃ khayā anāsavaṃ cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ diṭṭheva dhamme sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā upasampajja viharati.*
> "With the destruction of the influxes, he dwells having realized here and now the influx-free liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom."

May all beings cross the flood. May all beings realize the stilling of the mind.

## Plain-English Notes

### Summary Glossary

**Nibbāna** — Extinction, the going out of the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion. The mind stilled.

**Saṅkhāra** — Preparations, formations, volitional activities.

**Upadhi** — Assets, substrata clung to as supports for identity.

**Taṇhā** — Craving, thirst, the cause of suffering.

**Virāga** — Detachment, the fading away of lust.

**Nirodha** — Cessation, the stopping of the process of clinging.

**Viññāṇa** — Consciousness, arising dependent on conditions.

**Anidassana** — Non-manifestative, consciousness without an object.

**Appatiṭṭhita** — Unestablished, consciousness without standing.

**Upādāna** — Grasping, fuel. Four types: sense-desires, views, rites-and-rituals, self-doctrine.

**Āsava** — Influxes. Three types: sensuality, existence, ignorance.

**Sītibhūta** — Become cool, the Arahant's state.

**Tādi** — The Such-like One, unshaken by worldly conditions.

**Dukkha** — Suffering, unsatisfactoriness.

**Anattā** — Not-self.

**Anicca** — Impermanence.

**Paṭicca Samuppāda** — Dependent arising, the middle way.

**Nāma-rūpa** — Name-and-form, support of consciousness.

**Papañca** — Conceptual proliferation.

**Asmimāna** — The conceit "I am."

**Aññā** — Final knowledge, immediate certitude.

## Meditations

### The Final Letting Go

Sit quietly. Bring to mind all that you have learned — every sermon, every teaching, every insight. Now let it all go. Do not hold even the most profound realization. Simply sit in the stillness that remains when all grasping has ceased.

If a thought arises, let it pass. If an insight arises, let it pass. If a sense of peace arises, let it pass. Do not hold anything. Be like a room with all doors and windows open — the wind passes through freely, leaving no trace.

### The Thirty-Three Reflections

1. The theme verse — this peace is the stilling.
2. Craving is the origin — see it, and it ceases.
3. The fires burn from fuel — remove the fuel.
4. All is burning.
5. Preparations construct the world.
6. Proliferation entangles.
7. Impermanence — nothing lasts.
8. Not-self — nothing owns.
9. Detachment — the natural result of seeing.
10. Assets relinquished — nothing to hold.
11-16. Dependent arising — the middle way.
17. The aggregates — the burden.
18. Feeling without craving — possible.
19-20. The four truths — the map.
21. Mindfulness — the direct path.
22-27. The gradual training — the unfolding.
28. Consciousness unestablished — freedom.
29. Four graspings as fuel — all burning.
30. Middle way — beyond existence and non-existence.
31. Influxes destroyed — the root cut.
32. The Arahant's freedom — cool and released.
33. The mind stilled — here and now.

### The Nīla Contemplation

Imagine the sea of preparations — waves of thought, currents of feeling, tides of becoming. Now imagine the iron bar of wisdom — the direct seeing of things as they are. With one blow, the waters part. You see the bottom. You see Nibbāna. Not as a distant goal, but as the ground beneath all experience. Can you see it now?

### The River and the Sea

Reflect on your practice as a river flowing to the sea. Each moment of mindfulness is a tributary. Each insight is a current. The river flows inexorably. It does not need to reach the sea; it is already made of the same water. The path and the goal are not two.

### The Mind Stilled

Take the theme verse as your final meditation. Recite it slowly, phrase by phrase:

*Etaṃ santaṃ* — This is peaceful. Rest in that peace.

*Etaṃ paṇītaṃ* — This is excellent. Rest in that excellence.

*Sabbasaṅkhārasamatho* — The stilling of all preparations. Let all preparations cease.

*Sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo* — The relinquishment of all assets. Let all holdings go.

*Taṇhakkhayo* — The destruction of craving. Let all thirst end.

*Virāgo* — Detachment. Let all passion fade.

*Nirodho* — Cessation. Let all clinging stop.

*Nibbānaṃ* — Extinction. Let the fires go out.

The entire Dhamma is contained in this single verse. When the mind is stilled, Nibbāna is realized. The path is the goal. The tangle is disentangled. The river has reached the sea. The examination is complete, and the certitude is immediate.
