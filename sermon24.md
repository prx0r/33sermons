# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 24

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Man Shot with a Poison Arrow

A man is shot with a poison arrow. His companions rush to bring a surgeon. But the man refuses treatment until he knows: What is the archer's name? What is his clan? His village? Was the bow made of a tree or bamboo? Was the bowstring made of fibre or sinew or hemp? Was the arrow made of reed or sapling? With what kind of feathers was it fletched? With what kind of sinew was it bound? What species of tree did the arrow come from?

He would die before getting answers. And he would die foolishly — not because the answers are unknowable, but because they are irrelevant to the urgent task of removing the arrow.

The holy life, the Buddha says, is not for answering such questions. The holy life is for understanding suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path leading to its cessation. Everything else is secondary.

### Pariyāya and Nippariyāya

The Buddha's teaching has two levels of meaning: *pariyāya* — indirect, figurative, or provisional meaning — and *nippariyāya* — direct, ultimate meaning. The speculative questions belong to the realm of *pariyāya*; they can be discussed, but they do not touch the heart of the teaching.

The *nippariyāya* teaching is simple and direct: there is suffering, there is a cause, there is cessation, there is a path. This is what needs to be understood. The poison arrow is in your flesh right now — the arrow of birth, aging, sickness, and death. Every moment spent on metaphysical speculation is a moment the poison spreads.

### The Urgency of Practice

*Appamāda* — diligence — is the watchword. The house is on fire. Do you stand outside arguing about the chemical composition of the flames, or do you run for your life? The Dhamma calls for urgency. Not because death is coming — that is certain — but because the opportunity for practice in this human life is rare and precious.

The poison arrow is in you. Let the surgeon pull it out. The rest can wait — or need never be known at all.

## Plain-English Notes

### salla (Arrow/Dart)
The poisoned arrow of suffering. Its poison is craving; its wound is the cycle of birth and death.

### pariyāya / nippariyāya
Indirect and direct meaning. The Buddha used both modes: provisional teachings for those not yet ready, and direct, ultimate teachings for those who could see clearly.

### appamāda (Diligence)
Heedfulness, earnestness, urgency. The quality of mind that does not waste time on what is irrelevant to liberation.

---

## Meditations

### The Urgency of the Present Wound

Sit quietly and ask yourself: "What is the arrow in my experience right now?" Is it a bodily pain? A mental anxiety? A sense of dissatisfaction? Instead of asking why it is there or who caused it, attend to the direct experience of the wound. The surgeon's task is to remove the arrow, not to write its biography. Your task is to see the suffering and incline the mind toward its cessation.

**The logic this reveals:** Speculation is a distraction from direct investigation. The path is practical, not theoretical — it is about the here and now of suffering and its end.
