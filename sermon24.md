# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 24

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Man Shot with a Poison Arrow

A man is shot with a poison-tipped arrow. The wound is deep, the poison is spreading. His friends rush to bring a surgeon. But the man refuses treatment until his questions are answered: What is the archer's name? What is his clan? What is his village? Was the bow made of a tree or bamboo? Was the bowstring fibre, sinew, or hemp? Was the arrow shaft reed or sapling? With what feathers was it fletched — vulture, heron, hawk, or peacock? With what sinew was it bound — ox, buffalo, or deer? From what species of tree was the arrow taken?

He would die before getting answers. And he would die foolishly — not because the answers are unknowable, but because they are irrelevant to the urgent task of removing the arrow. The holy life, the Buddha declares, does not depend on answering whether the world is eternal or not eternal, whether the world is finite or infinite, whether the Tathāgata exists after death or does not exist. The arrow of craving and suffering is in your flesh right now. Every moment spent on metaphysical speculation is a moment the poison spreads further through the bloodstream. Let the surgeon pull it out.

### Pariyāya and Nippariyāya

The Buddha's teaching has two levels of meaning: *pariyāya* — indirect, figurative, or provisional meaning — and *nippariyāya* — direct, ultimate meaning. The speculative questions — the famous ten undeclared points — belong to the realm of *pariyāya*. They can be discussed in philosophical circles, debated in halls, pondered in solitude, but they do not touch the heart of the teaching.

The *nippariyāya* teaching is the poison arrow teaching, and it is direct: there is suffering, there is a cause, there is cessation, there is a path. This is what needs to be seen. The Buddha is like a surgeon who does not ask how the arrow was made — he removes it. The Dhamma is the surgeon's hand. Do not interrogate the hand; let it do its work.

### The Urgency of Practice

*Appamāda* — diligence, heedfulness, earnestness — is the watchword of the entire teaching. Like a man whose turban is on fire: he does not stop to ask about the fire's chemical composition, who lit it, how long it has been burning, or whether it is a real fire or an illusion. He tears the turban off and extinguishes it. There is only the urgency of the burn.

The poison arrow is in you. The turban is ablaze. The house of existence is burning on all sides. The Dhamma calls for urgency — not because death is coming, which is certain, but because the opportunity for practice in this human life is rare and precious. The questions can wait — or need never be answered at all. The removal of the arrow cannot wait.

## Plain-English Notes

### salla (Arrow / Dart)
The poisoned arrow of suffering. Its poison is craving; its wound is the cycle of birth and death.

### pariyāya / nippariyāya
Indirect and direct meaning. The Buddha used both modes: provisional teachings for those not yet ready, and direct, ultimate teachings for those who could see clearly. The poison arrow teaching is nippariyāya — remove the arrow now.

### appamāda (Diligence)
Heedfulness, earnestness, urgency. Like a man whose turban is on fire — he does not ask questions; he acts. This is the quality of mind that wastes no time on irrelevancies.

### ten questions (avyākata)
The speculative questions the Buddha left unanswered — the archer's name, clan, village, bow, arrow — all irrelevant to the task of removing the arrow of suffering.

## Meditations

### The Urgency of the Present Wound

Sit quietly and ask yourself: "What is the arrow in my experience right now?" Is it a bodily pain? A mental anxiety? A sense of dissatisfaction, restlessness, or unease? Instead of asking why it is there — who caused it, what past action brought it, whether it is fair or deserved — attend to the direct experience of the wound. Feel the sting of it. The surgeon's task is to remove the arrow, not to write its biography. Your task is to see the suffering clearly and incline the mind toward its cessation. The turban is on fire — do not ask about the flame. Put it out.

### Letting the Surgeon Work

Imagine yourself on the surgeon's bench. The arrow is in your shoulder. The surgeon's hand is steady, the knife is ready. Your job is not to instruct the surgeon or to question his methods. Your job is to be still and let the work be done. The Dhamma is that surgeon. Trust the practice. Let the arrow be removed.

**The logic this reveals:** Speculation is a distraction from direct investigation. The path is practical, not theoretical — it is about the here and now of suffering and its end.
