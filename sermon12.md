# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 12

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*

---

## The Teaching

### Rūpa: That Which Is Affected

The Buddha gave a new etymological explanation to the word rūpa in the Khajjanīyasutta:

> *Ruppatīti kho, bhikkhave, tasmā rūpan'ti vuccati. Kenā ruppati? Sītena uṇhena jighacchāya pipāsāya ḍaṃsamakasavātātapasarīsapasamphassena.*
>
> "It is affected, monks, that is why it is called form. By what is it affected? By cold, heat, hunger, thirst, and the sting of gadflies, mosquitoes, wind, sun, and creeping things."

This is the defining characteristic of form (rūpa): it is what gets affected. It is the vulnerable, the exposed, the touchable. The etymology ruppati — to be affected, to be stirred, to be changed — shows that form is not a static substance but a process of being affected.

### Contact: Verbal and Resistance Impressions

Contact arises dependent on name-and-form. Form gets a "verbal impression" by the naming quality in name, and name gets a "resistance-impression" by the striking quality in form.

When a name is applied to a form, the form receives a verbal impression — it becomes "this," "that," "a table," "a person." When form resists, name receives a resistance-impression — the bump, the pressure, the impact. Contact, properly so-called in this Dhamma, is the combination of these two: verbal impression and resistance-impression.

### The Worldling's Conclusion

The worldling comes to a conclusion:

> *Disvā bhavaṃ bhavañca, vinicchayaṃ kurute jantu loke.*
>
> "Having seen the existence and destruction of material forms, a man in this world comes to a conclusion."

The worldling sees forms appearing and disappearing and draws conclusions — eternalism or nihilism. But the Buddha's teaching avoids both: see the arising as arising, the ceasing as ceasing, without adding a conclusion.

### The Cessation of Contact

A series of questions and answers in the suttas reveals the condition for contact:

> "Dependent on name-and-form is contact. In the absence of what, do contacts not bring about contact?"

The answer: when name-and-form cease, contact ceases. And what must cease for name-and-form to cease? Consciousness. And what for consciousness? Preparations. Back through the chain until ignorance. The entire chain is visible in the simple fact of contact — here and now, not in past lives.

## Plain-English Notes

### Rūpa as "Being Affected"

The word rūpa is usually translated as "form" or "matter." But the Buddha's own etymology is more revealing: rūpa is what *ruppati* — what is affected, stirred, vexed. Form is not a block of stuff. It's vulnerability. The body is not a thing — it's a being-affected-by-cold, -by-heat, -by-hunger, -by-mosquitoes.

### Two Kinds of Impression

Contact = verbal impression (from name) + resistance impression (from form). The name gives the "what" — the form gives the "that." Together they produce contact — the meeting of sense and object.

## Meditation

### The Vulnerability Meditation

Sit and feel your body not as a solid thing but as a site of being-affected. Feel cold touching the skin. Feel pressure from the chair. Feel the itch of a mosquito bite (or the memory of one). Each of these is rūpa in action — being affected.

Now notice: the "affectedness" is not something that happens to rūpa. It *is* rūpa. Rūpa is the being-affected. There is no "body" behind the affectedness that receives the impacts. The body is the receiving.

**The logic this reveals:** When you look for the body as a thing, you find only being-affected. Rūpa is not a substance — it's a process. The Arahant sees this and no longer identifies with the process.
