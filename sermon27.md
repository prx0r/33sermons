# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 27

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Thirty-Three Synonyms for Nibbāna

In the Asaṅkhatasaṃyutta, the Buddha gives thirty-three synonyms for Nibbāna. It is called the unconditioned (*asaṅkhata*), the end (*anta*), the devoid (*anāsava*), the truth (*sacca*), the farther shore (*pāra*), the subtle (*nipuṇa*), the very hard to see (*sududdasa*), the unaging (*ajara*), the stable (*dhuva*), the undisintegrating (*apalokita*), the unmanifest (*anidassana*), the non-proliferating (*nippapañca*), the deathless (*amata*), the peaceful (*santa*), the auspicious (*siva*), the secure (*khema*), the destruction of craving (*taṇhakkhaya*), the wonderful (*acchariya*), the unailing (*ābādha*), the Nibbāna element (*nibbānadhātu*).

Each of these words is a pointer, a signpost. None of them is Nibbāna itself. They are like fingers pointing at the moon — if you grasp the finger, you miss the moon.

### The Danger of Reifying Synonyms

The danger is that we take these synonyms as descriptions of an entity — a metaphysical something that exists independently. "The deathless" — we imagine a state of eternal life. "The unconditioned" — we imagine a transcendent realm. "The secure" — we imagine a safe haven.

But these are all *pariyāya* — figurative expressions pointing to the cessation of suffering. Nibbāna is not a place, not a thing, not a being. It is the end of becoming, the stilling of preparations, the relinquishment of all assets. The synonyms are not definitions; they are therapeutic tools, each designed to counter a particular clinging.

### Saṅkhata and Asaṅkhata

Everything we can conceive — every thought, every feeling, every perception — is *saṅkhata*, conditioned. It arises, persists, and ceases. Nibbāna is *asaṅkhata* — not because it is a special kind of conditioned thing, but because it is the cessation of the conditioned. It is not a thing among things. It is not an object of thought.

*Vivattamāna* — "not rolling up" or "not manifesting" — is another synonym. Nibbāna does not appear as a phenomenon because it is the stopping of the rolling of saṃsāra. Like the ocean that does not rise into waves when there is no wind, Nibbāna is the stilling of all preparations.

---

## Plain-English Notes

### asaṅkhata (Unconditioned)
Not made, not compounded, not produced by causes. Nibbāna is unconditioned, but this does not make it a "thing" — it is the cessation of all conditioned phenomena.

### saṅkhata (Conditioned)
All phenomena that arise dependent on causes. The entire world of experience — mental and physical — is conditioned.

### pariyāya (Synonym / Figurative Expression)
A way of speaking, an indirect designation. The thirty-three synonyms are not literal descriptions but skilful means to direct the mind toward the cessation of suffering.

### vivattamāna (Not Rolling Up)
A rare and profound synonym: that which does not roll up or manifest, referring to the cessation of the turning of saṃsāra.

## Meditations

### Looking Beyond the Finger

Take one synonym — "the deathless" (*amata*). Sit with the word and notice what images arise: eternal life, a heavenly realm, the survival of a soul. Now let all that go. Instead, feel the direct longing for freedom from death — the fear, the hope, the grasping. Now see that "deathless" is not about endless living but about the cessation of the very notion of death. The deathless is here, in the stilling of the mind that grasps at life and recoils from death. Can you touch that stillness now?

**The logic this reveals:** All synonyms for Nibbāna are therapeutic counters to specific defilements. They point, but they are not the pointed-to. The reality is to be seen, not named.
