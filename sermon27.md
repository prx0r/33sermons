# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 27

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Thirty-Three Synonyms as Pointers

In the Asaṅkhatasaṃyutta, the Buddha gives thirty-three synonyms for Nibbāna: the unconditioned (*asaṅkhata*), the end (*anta*), the devoid (*anāsava*), the truth (*sacca*), the farther shore (*pāra*), the subtle (*nipuṇa*), the very hard to see (*sududdasa*), the unaging (*ajara*), the stable (*dhuva*), the undisintegrating (*apalokita*), the unmanifest (*anidassana*), the non-proliferating (*nippapañca*), the deathless (*amata*), the peaceful (*santa*), the auspicious (*siva*), the secure (*khema*), the destruction of craving (*taṇhakkhaya*), the wonderful (*acchariya*), the unailing (*ābādha*), the Nibbāna element (*nibbānadhātu*), and others.

Each of these words is a pointer, a signpost. They are like different fingers pointing to the moon. One finger points from the left, another from the right, another from below — each gives a different angle, a different perspective. But none of them is the moon itself. If you grasp the finger, you miss the moon entirely.

The danger is taking any synonym as describing an entity — a metaphysical something that exists independently. "The deathless" — we imagine a state of eternal life. "The unconditioned" — we imagine a transcendent realm beyond space and time. "The farther shore" — we imagine a place across a river. But all these are *pariyāya* — figurative expressions pointing to the cessation of suffering, not descriptions of a thing. Each synonym is a therapeutic tool, designed to counter a particular clinging. "The deathless" counters the fear of death. "The secure" counters the anxiety of insecurity. "The stable" counters the despair of impermanence. They are medicine, not metaphysics.

### The Raft Reprise

Even the word "Nibbāna" itself is a raft — a designation, a convenient label, not a thing-in-itself. The raft simile from Sermon 23 applies here with undiminished force. If you cling to "Nibbāna" as a concept — if you defend it, define it, and argue about it — you are hoisting the raft onto your head. If you take "the unconditioned" as a doctrinal position to hold against others, you are grasping the snake by the wrong end.

The Dhamma is like a medicinal herb. You do not need to know its botanical classification, its Latin name, its place in the plant kingdom, or its chemical composition. You need to take it for your illness. The synonyms are like different descriptions of the herb's taste, colour, smell, and effects — they help you recognize it and use it correctly, but they are not the herb. A man dying of a snakebite does not pause to classify the antidote; he drinks it. The Dhamma is for drinking, not for classifying.

### Asaṅkhata and Saṅkhata

Everything we can conceive — every thought, every feeling, every perception, every mental image — is *saṅkhata*, conditioned. It arises, persists for a moment, and ceases. Nibbāna is *asaṅkhata* — unconditioned. But this does not mean Nibbāna is a special kind of conditioned thing that happens to be labelled "unconditioned." It is not an object of thought at all.

The unconditioned is like the absence of a fire. When the fuel has run out, when the wood is ash and the flame is gone, there is an absence. That absence is not a positive reality — it is not a "thing" that exists somewhere else. It is the sheer fact that the conditions for burning are no longer present. So too with Nibbāna: it is not a thing that exists somewhere in a transcendent realm. It is the cessation of the conditions that produce suffering. *Vivattamāna* — "not rolling up" — is another profound synonym. Nibbāna does not appear as a phenomenon because it is the stopping of the rolling of saṃsāra. Like the ocean that does not rise into waves when there is no wind, Nibbāna is the stilling of all preparations. The synonyms are all fingers pointing at this stillness — but the stillness itself must be seen, not named.

## Plain-English Notes

### asaṅkhata (Unconditioned)
Not made, not compounded, not produced by causes. Like the absence of a fire — not a positive reality, but the cessation of burning. The sheer fact that the fuel has run out.

### saṅkhata (Conditioned)
All phenomena that arise dependent on causes. The entire world of experience — mental and physical — is conditioned, including every thought about the unconditioned.

### Nibbāna as raft
Even the word "Nibbāna" is a raft — a designation, not a thing-in-itself. To be used, then abandoned.

### Dhamma as medicinal herb
You do not need to classify it. You need to take it for your illness. The synonyms are like descriptions of the herb's taste, colour, and effects.

### pariyāya (Synonym / Figurative Expression)
The thirty-three synonyms are like fingers pointing to the moon — each points from a different angle, but none is the moon itself.

### vivattamāna (Not Rolling Up)
That which does not roll up or manifest — the cessation of the turning of saṃsāra.

## Meditations

### Looking Beyond the Finger

Take one synonym — "the deathless" (*amata*). Sit with the word and notice what images arise: eternal life, a heavenly realm, the survival of a soul, consciousness continuing forever. Now let all that go. Instead, feel the direct longing for freedom from death — the fear, the hope, the grasping at continuity. See that "deathless" is not about endless living. It is about the cessation of the very notion of death. The deathless is here, in the stilling of the mind that grasps at life and recoils from death. Can you touch that stillness now, without naming it, without holding it as a thing?

### Taking the Medicine

Consider a teaching you have studied and understood intellectually — perhaps dependent arising or not-self. Now ask: have you taken it as medicine, or have you merely classified it? The botanist who names every herb but never swallows one dies of the same disease as the ignorant. Let the Dhamma enter you not as knowledge to be held, but as medicine to be taken.

**The logic this reveals:** All synonyms for Nibbāna are therapeutic counters to specific defilements. They point, but they are not the pointed-to. The reality is to be seen, not named. Even the word "Nibbāna" is a raft — to be used, then abandoned.
