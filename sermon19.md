# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon XIX

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Fire Embers Analogy

Consider a blazing fire. When no more firewood is added, the flames subside but the logs already burning continue smouldering as embers. Eventually even those embers cool to ashes. This is the nature of the two Nibbāna elements.

The living Arahant is like the smouldering embers. Lust, hate, and delusion no longer flare up — no new fuel of craving is added. But the body — the residual fuel — is still there, still producing pleasant and painful feelings through the six sense spheres. The fires are out, but the embers remain warm. This is *sa-upādisesā Nibbānadhātu* — the Nibbāna element with residual clinging.

At death, even the embers cool completely. The body, that last residue of past grasping, is abandoned. This is *anupādisesā Nibbānadhātu* — the Nibbāna element without residual clinging.

### Saṅkhārā as a Floral Design

To understand this more deeply, we must consider the nature of *saṅkhārā* — preparations. Saṅkhārā are like a floral design. Imagine an intricate, beautiful arrangement of flowers — petals, leaves, colours all held together in a pattern. What holds them in place? Glue. That glue is *taṇhā* — craving. While craving is present, the design holds its shape. But when the glue dries, the design falls apart. Each petal returns to being just a petal. There is no "design" apart from the arrangement.

The Arahant has seen that all preparations are held together only by the glue of craving. When that glue has dried — when craving is destroyed — the design is seen for what it is: a mere conjunction of conditions with no substance behind it. This insight cuts through the illusion of a solid self. Just as the floral design has no independent existence apart from its components, so too the sense of "I" has no existence apart from the aggregates that compose it. The drying of the glue is the destruction of craving — and with that, the design collapses of its own accord.

### The Ball of Iron and the Flame

Form and the formless are closely intertwined. Consider a ball of iron heated red-hot in a furnace. The ball of iron is like the flame of fire — and vice versa. You cannot separate the heat from the iron while the iron is hot. The form (the iron ball) and the formless (the heat, the flame) are one phenomenon.

This illustrates the relationship between *nāma* (mentality) and *rūpa* (materiality) — they are not two separate things but two aspects of the same dependently arisen process. The Arahant sees through this entanglement. He does not mistake the body for a self, nor does he mistake consciousness for a self. He sees that "form" and "formless" are conceptual designations overlaying a single causal process. The ball of iron is hot — but there is no "hot" apart from the iron, and no "iron" apart from the heat. So too with the Arahant's understanding of the psychophysical complex: there is no experiencer apart from experiencing, no knower apart from knowing.

### The Tortoise and the Frog

The commentarial tradition has offered similes to describe Nibbāna, but some are inadequate. There is the parable of the tortoise pulling its limbs within its shell — used to suggest that the sage withdraws into a state of inner stillness. And the parable of the frog that has never seen land — used to suggest that Nibbāna is utterly beyond ordinary experience.

Ñāṇananda critiques these similes. They are inadequate because they conceive Nibbāna as a *state* — like a trance, a meditative absorption (*jhāna*), a withdrawal into some special mode of consciousness. The tortoise-withdrawn-into-its-shell is still a tortoise; it has merely pulled in its limbs. The frog that has never seen land is still a frog submerged in water; its ignorance of land does not make it enlightened.

Nibbāna is not a state among states. It is the cessation of the very conditions that make states possible. These similes still assume a "one who has attained" and a "state that is attained." They assume a subject who experiences and an object that is experienced. But Nibbāna is the end of the subject-object framework itself. The tortoise and the frog similes, while well-intentioned, inadvertently reinforce the very dichotomy they seek to transcend. They describe a withdrawal *within* experience, not the cessation of the conditions for experience altogether.

## Plain-English Notes

### The Two Nibbāna Elements

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Sa-upādisesā Nibbānadhātu | The Arahant's living experience — the body remains (embers), but no new fuel is added |
| Anupādisesā Nibbānadhātu | Final cessation at death — even the embers have cooled |

These are not two separate Nibbānas. They are one extinguishment viewed from two temporal standpoints: while the body still lingers and after it has ceased. The fire is the same fire; only the presence or absence of residual heat differs.

### Upādi / Upādiṇṇa

The body is "residual fuel" — it was "grasped" by past craving (*upādāna*). The Arahant does not grasp it anew. He bears it like a borrowed item, using it without claiming ownership.

The distinction between *upādiṇṇa* (the body as "clung-to") and *upādāna* (the act of clinging) is essential. The body of the Arahant is still "clung-to" in the sense that past craving produced it, but there is no present act of clinging. The residual fuel burns without being fed.

### Saṅkhārā as Preparations

All conditioned phenomena — mental and physical — are "preparations." They are put together, arranged, fabricated. Like a floral design, they have no substance apart from their conditions.

## Meditations

### The Embers Contemplation

Sit and bring to mind a desire that still burns in you — something you crave. Feel its heat. Now imagine that desire completely extinguished — no fuel, no fire, coolness. But the body remains. You still breathe, eat, feel pleasure and pain. The fire is out but the embers are warm.

Now consider death: when the body is gone, even the embers cool. Not a new state — just the complete cooling of what had already been extinguished. The two Nibbāna elements are not two destinations. They are the same extinguishment seen from two angles.

**The logic this reveals:** Nibbāna is not a future promise. The extinguishment is here, in the not-feeding of craving. What comes after death is only the body's final cooling of what the mind has already cooled.
