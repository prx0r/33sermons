# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon XIX

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Farther Shore: A Psychological Understanding

Many discourses dealing with Nibbāna have been misinterpreted due to a lack of appreciation of the fact that the transcendence of the world and crossing over to the farther shore have to be understood in a psychological sense. The view that the arahant at the end of his life enters into an absolutely existing *asaṅkhata dhātu* — an 'unprepared element' — seems to have gained acceptance in the commentarial period. But some of the discourses cited in support of that view deal, on the contrary, with a realization the arahant goes through here and now, in this very life — a realization of the cessation of existence, or the cessation of the six sense-spheres.

Even when the Buddha refers to the arahant as the Brahmin who, having gone beyond, is standing on the farther shore, he was speaking of the arahant who has realized, in this very life, the influx-free deliverance of the mind and deliverance through wisdom, in his concentration of the fruit of arahantship.

### The Nālakasutta: Not Twice, Not Once

The Nālakasutta in the Sutta Nipāta contains a verse that was the subject of much controversy among the ancients:

> *Uccāvacā hi paṭipadā, samaṇena pakāsitā, na pāraṃ diguṇaṃ yanti, na idaṃ ekaguṇaṃ mutaṃ.*
>
> "High and low are the paths, made known by the recluse. They go not twice to the farther shore, nor yet is it to be reckoned a going once."

This verse is puzzling. If the arahant goes to the farther shore, why does the text say "not twice" and "not once"? The answer lies in understanding the farther shore psychologically. The crossing is not a literal journey to another place. It is the cessation of the very dichotomy of "this shore" and "farther shore." The arahant does not go twice — he does not return to existence. But neither does he go once — for there is no going at all. The crossing is the end of the very notion of crossing.

### The Paramaṭṭhakasutta: The Steadfast One Does Not Return

The Paramaṭṭhakasutta closes with the line: *pāraṃgato na pacceti tādi* — "the steadfast one, once gone to the farther shore, does not come back." The word *tādi* means "such-like," the one who is steady, unchanging in his realization. He does not come back — not because he has gone somewhere else, but because the condition for returning (craving, existence) has been extinguished.

The steadfast one does not "return" to any state of existence because he has seen through existence itself. His "going beyond" is the end of the worldly perspective that posits a shore at all.

## Plain-English Notes

### The Farther Shore (Pārimaṃ Tīraṃ)

Not a place after death. The "farther shore" is Nibbāna — but Nibbāna is not a location. It is the cessation of the mental construction that divides experience into "this side" (existence) and "that side" (non-existence). The crossing is psychological: from identification with the five aggregates to freedom from them.

### Pāraṃgato — Gone Beyond

The arahant is one who has "gone beyond." This is not a spatial movement. It is the transcendence of the three realms of existence — kāma, rūpa, arūpa — through direct knowledge. He has "gone beyond" the tendency to grasp at any form of existence.

### Tādi — The Steadfast One

*Tādi* is a key term for the arahant. It means "such-like" — unchanging, steady, not blown about by worldly conditions. The tādi is not affected by praise or blame, gain or loss. He does not come back because he no longer inclines towards any form of existence.

## Meditations

### The Shorelessness Contemplation

Sit and consider the notion of "this shore" — where you are now, in the world of the five aggregates. Then consider the "farther shore" — Nibbāna, release. Ask yourself: is the farther shore a *place*? Can you travel to Nibbāna?

Notice how the mind wants to make release into a destination, a location "out there." Now ask: what if the distinction between this shore and that shore is itself the problem? The arahant does not go. He ceases to stand on either shore.

**The logic this reveals:** The farther shore is not reached by going. It is reached by seeing through the dichotomy of here and there, existence and non-existence. Crossing is the end of the shore-concept itself.
