# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 13

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*

---

## The Teaching

### Papañca: Conceptual Proliferation

This is the twelfth sermon in the series on Nibbāna. We bring up the two terms papañca and nippapañca, which help us rediscover quite a deep dimension in Buddhist philosophy, hidden under the sense of time.

Though in ordinary linguistic usage papañca meant 'elaboration', 'circumlocution', and 'verbosity', the Madhupiṇḍikasutta has shown us that in the context of sensory perception it has some special significance. It portrays how a person, directed by sense perception, is overwhelmed by *papañcasaññāsaṅkhā* with regard to sense-objects relating to the three periods of time — past, present, and future — as a result of indulging in papañca based on reasoning about percepts.

Papañca has connotations of some kind of delusion, obsession, and confusion arising in a man's mind due to sense perception. The Madhupiṇḍikasutta shows that this elaboration spans the three periods of time. When perception proliferates, it does not stay in the present — it reaches into the past ("it was like this") and the future ("it will be like that"). This spanning of time is the very essence of papañca. The present moment is lost in the elaboration of what was and what will be.

> *Kenaṭṭhena papañco? Mattapamattākārapāpanaṭṭhena papañco.*
>
> "Papañca in what sense? In the sense that it leads one on to a state of intoxication and indolence."

Papañca is not just "conceptual proliferation" in a neutral sense. It is the compulsive, intoxicating elaboration of experience into a world of self and other, mine and yours. It is what happens when perception is not seen as perception but is taken as reality.

---

### The Madhupiṇḍikasutta Analysis

The Madhupiṇḍikasutta shows how papañca operates: through contact, feeling, perception, and then the proliferation of perceptions regarding past, present, and future sense-objects. The chain is:

1. Contact → Feeling → Perception
2. Perception → Papañcasaññāsaṅkhā (perceptions of proliferation)
3. These perceptions overwhelm the person regarding past, present, and future sense-objects

The person does not just see what is present. He elaborates it with memories of the past and anticipations of the future. This elaboration is papañca — the construction of a world beyond the bare given.

### Nippapañca: Without Proliferation

The Arahant, on the other hand, is nippapañca — without proliferation. He sees what is present without elaborating it into a past and future, without adding the intoxicated "I" and "mine." The world is seen as it is, without the obsessive elaboration that characterizes the worldling's perception.

The Buddha gives a significant piece of advice to the monk in higher training: regarding his world-view, he may think "this is mine," but he should not be vain about it — *tena ca mā maññi*. Why? Because it should be well comprehended by him. The world-view itself is a training tool, not a final possession. Even the right view must be held lightly, without the intoxication of "I have understood."

## Plain-English Notes

### Papañca: Intoxicated Elaboration

Not just "thinking too much." Papañca is the intoxicated state where perception elaborates experience into a full-blown world. You see a person → you elaborate: who they are, what they said yesterday, what they might say tomorrow, whether they like you, whether you like them. The bare perception is lost in the elaboration.

The word is related to "mad" (as in madana — intoxication). Papañca is perception drunk on itself.

### Nippapañca: The Sober State

The Arahant's perception is nippapañca — not because he doesn't think, but because thinking no longer elaborates into an intoxicated world. He sees the person as a person, without the obsessive elaboration. Perception serves its function without building a world of self and other.

## Meditation

### The Elaboration Awareness

Sit and notice a simple perception — a sound, a sight, a touch. Now watch how the mind elaborates. The sound becomes "a bird" → "a sparrow" → "there's a nest somewhere" → "I remember hearing that sound last spring" → "I like that sound" → "I wish I was outside." In seconds, the bare perception has proliferated into a world.

Practice: catch the moment of elaboration. Between the bare sound and the story, there's a gap. In that gap, see the intoxicated quality of mind. Just see it. Don't suppress it.

**The logic this reveals:** The Arahant has stopped the elaboration. Not by blocking perception, but by no longer being intoxicated by it. Perception occurs without papañca — it's like a sober person watching others drunk.
