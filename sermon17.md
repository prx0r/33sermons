# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 17

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### Papañca and Nippapañca: The Gap

Between the sensory experience of the worldling and the wisdom of the Arahant lies a vast gap — the gap between *papañca* (conceptual proliferation) and *nippapañca* (non-proliferation). In worldly sensory experience, there is always discrimination: a thing to be grasped and one who grasps, a subject and an object. This bifurcation is the mark of *papañca*. If there is a seen, there must be something seen and one who sees — that is the logic of the worldling's mind.

### The Seen Without the Seer

The Bāhiyasutta overturns this logic. When the Buddha says "in the seen there will be just the seen," he points to a mode of experience in which the subject-object dichotomy collapses. There is nothing to see, no one to see — only "a seen." The compact mass of "I" and "my world" breaks open into a field of bare phenomena.

### The Collocation of Conditions

The seen, the heard, the sensed, and the cognized are not "things" in the solid sense that the worldling imagines. To the wisdom eye of the Arahant, they appear as mere conglomerations of conditions, dependent on contact (*phassam paṭicca*). Conditions come together for a moment, then disperse. There is no core, no substance, no self behind them.

This insight into dependence on contact is the very essence of dependent arising (*paṭicca samuppāda*). To see that the seen is just a momentary collocation of conditions — eye, visible object, consciousness — is to see the Dhamma itself.

### Viññāṇaṃ Anidassanaṃ

To transcend the narrow perspective limited to the six sense spheres, one must realize what the Buddha called *viññāṇaṃ anidassanaṃ, anantaṃ sabbato pabhaṃ* — "consciousness which is non-manifestative, endless, lustrous on all sides." This is consciousness that does not take a stand anywhere, does not land on any object, does not manifest as "I see" or "I hear." It is the consciousness of the Arahant that has seen the cessation of contact.

The cessation of contact is the key to Nibbāna. When contact ceases — not by suppression but by full comprehension — the entire edifice of *papañca* collapses. Without contact, the seen no longer stands as "seen," the hearer no longer stands as "hearer." What remains is non-manifestative consciousness, which is Nibbāna.

## Plain-English Notes

### Papañca (Conceptual Proliferation)

The mind's compulsive tendency to split experience into subject and object, self and world, thing and owner. It is not merely thinking too much but the structural fragmentation of reality into a perceiver and perceived.

### Nippapañca (Non-Proliferation)

The Arahant's mode of experience where the subject-object split is healed. Perception occurs without the construction of a self behind it or a world in front of it.

### Phassam Paṭicca (Dependent on Contact)

The key insight: all sensory experience is a momentary conjunction of conditions. Eye + visible object + eye-consciousness = contact. Nothing more. No "I" is needed for the equation.

### Viññāṇaṃ Anidassanaṃ (Non-Manifestative Consciousness)

Consciousness that does not "point to" or "manifest" any object. It does not take a stance anywhere. It is like space — luminous, unfixed, and free.

## Meditations

### Collapse the Subject-Object

Sit and attend to a visual object. Notice: there is "the object" and "me seeing it." Now investigate: can you find a "me" apart from the act of seeing? Where exactly is the border between the seen and the seer?

The truth is: the seer is just another thought, another phenomenon within the field. The subject is not outside the seen; it is part of the same collocation. When this is seen, the gap between subject and object closes.

**The logic this reveals:** The worldling's subject-object duality is a construction of *papañca*. When contact is seen as mere condition, the duality dissolves. Nibbāna is not a state *within* experience but the cessation of the framework that creates "experiencer" and "experienced."
