# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 17

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*
>
> "This is peaceful, this is excellent, namely the stilling of all preparations, the relinquishment of all assets, the destruction of craving, detachment, cessation, extinction."

---

## The Teaching

### The Gaze That Knows No Horizon

The Arahant's vision of voidness is like a penetrative gaze with no limit, no boundary. When one stands on a vast plain, the horizon seems to bound the view — but it is not a real limit; it is an artefact of the eye. Move forward, and the horizon recedes. There is no actual line where earth meets sky.

In the same way, the worldling's vision is bounded by *papañca* — conceptual proliferation that draws a line between self and world, subject and object. The Arahant's gaze knows no such horizon. He does not see "things" standing against a "self." The horizon of self and other, inside and outside, being and non-being — all have dissolved. What remains is a vision without limit, a seeing that does not land on any boundary.

### Papañca and Nippapañca

Between the sensory experience of the worldling and the wisdom of the Arahant lies the gap between *papañca* and *nippapañca*. In worldly experience, there is always discrimination: a thing to be grasped and one who grasps. This bifurcation is the mark of *papañca*.

### The Twig Fire

The Venerable Sāriputta once spoke of a small fire of twigs — easy to extinguish. The Arahant's defilements are like such a fire. A great bonfire of logs takes much water to quench, but a handful of twigs blazes up and dies down almost at once. The worldling's passions are a forest fire, fed by layer upon layer of *maññanā*. The Arahant's defilements, by contrast, are so attenuated that the slightest turning of the mind toward wisdom extinguishes them. They flare briefly, if at all, and subside without fuel.

### The Flame That Simulates Compactness

A flame looks like a solid, compact thing — a single, steady tongue of fire. But watch closely: it is constantly flickering, changing, each moment a new combustion. There is no "core" of fire that persists. Existence too looks solid — a self that endures, a world that stands firm — but it is impermanent through and through, a series of discrete moments burning and vanishing.

The worldling sees the flame as compact and grasps it as "I" and "mine." The Arahant sees the flickering — the momentariness — and does not mistake it for a solid thing. The compactness of self is an illusion, like the apparent solidity of a flame.

### The Firm Post at the City Gate

> *Indakhīlūpamo, bhikkhave, ariyasāvako hoti.*

The noble disciple — the Arahant — is like a firm post at the city gate (*indakhīla*). Such a post is deeply embedded in the earth, unmovable. The winds of praise and blame, gain and loss, fame and disrepute, pleasure and pain — all blow against it, but it does not tremble.

A lesser post, set shallowly, would shake with every gust. The worldling is like that — swayed by every opinion, every change of fortune. But the Arahant, having seen through the horizon of self, stands unmoved. Not because he suppresses feeling, but because he knows that the winds are just winds — they pass through the open space of his mind without meeting a wall.

### Viññāṇaṃ Anidassanaṃ

To transcend the limited perspective of the six sense spheres, one must realize *viññāṇaṃ anidassanaṃ, anantaṃ sabbato pabhaṃ* — consciousness that is non-manifestative, endless, lustrous on all sides. This is the gaze that knows no horizon. It does not land on any object, does not take a stand anywhere. In this consciousness, the compactness of existence is seen through, the twig fire of defilements is extinguished, and the firm post stands unwavering.

## Plain-English Notes

### The Horizonless Gaze

The Arahant's vision is not bounded by the self-reference that turns experience into "my experience." It is a seeing without a seer — limitless.

### The Twig Fire

The Arahant's defilements are so minimal they are like a handful of twigs — easily extinguished. This is not repression but the natural result of full comprehension.

### The Flame and Compactness

Nothing in existence is truly solid. The Arahant sees the flickering impermanence that the worldling mistakes for a stable self.

### Indakhīlūpamo — The Firm Post

Steadfastness that comes not from rigidity but from having no stake in the winds of the world. The post is firm because it is deeply rooted in wisdom.

---

## Meditations

### The Open Gaze

Sit and let your gaze rest on the horizon — real or imagined. Notice how the horizon is not a line but an endless recession. Now turn this gaze inward: can you look at experience without landing on any "thing"? Can you see without fixing on a seer?

### The Twig Fire

Bring to mind a small defilement — a flash of irritation, a flicker of desire. See it as a handful of twigs. How easily it burns out when you do not feed it. You do not need to extinguish it violently; just stop adding twigs.

**The logic this reveals:** The solidity of self and world is a simulation, like a flame's apparent compactness. When you see through it, the horizon dissolves, the fire goes out, and the post stands firm — not by effort, but by nature.
