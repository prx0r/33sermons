# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 11

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*

---

## The Teaching

### The Gem in the Pond: The Ummaggajātaka Parable

With the help of a parable based on the problem of the gem in the Ummaggajātaka, we made an attempt to clarify how the personality view arises due to the ignorance of the fact that name-and-form is something reflected on consciousness.

A certain would-be wise man took the trouble to empty a pond and even dig out the mud under the impression that there is actually a gem in it, simply because there appeared to be a gem reflected in the pond. Similarly, by taking to be real name-and-form, which is only an image reflected on consciousness, leading to a personality view (sakkāyadiṭṭhi), both eternalism and nihilism tend towards two extremes.

In reality, name-and-form is an image — a reflection. The personality view arises when this reflection is mistaken for a real thing. The reflection is not nothing — it appears. But it is not a self. It is like a reflection of a face in a mirror: the face is not in the mirror, but the reflection appears. To say "there is a face in the mirror" is wrong. To say "there is nothing there" is also wrong. The middle path: there is a reflection, dependently arisen.

Under the influence of self-love, eternalism took up the view that there is a self, and looked forward to its perpetuation. Prompted by self-hate, annihilationism cherished the fond hope that the release from this self will occur at death. Both these extreme views confuse the issue by not understanding the reflected image as such.

The middle path, which the Buddha introduced to the world, avoids these two extremes by offering a knowledge and vision of things as they are — *yathābhūtañāṇadassana* — in place of those two views of existence and non-existence.

---

### The "I Am" Arises Dependent on the Aggregates

The thought "I am" — *asmīti* — arises dependent on the five aggregates, not independently. The Buddha states:

> *Kiñca upādāya asmīti hoti, no anupādāya? Rūpaṃ upādāya asmīti hoti, no anupādāya; vedanaṃ upādāya asmīti hoti, no anupādāya; saññaṃ upādāya asmīti hoti, no anupādāya; saṅkhāre upādāya asmīti hoti, no anupādāya; viññāṇaṃ upādāya asmīti hoti, no anupādāya.*
>
> "Depending on what does the thought 'I am' occur, and not without depending? Depending on form, the thought 'I am' occurs, not without depending; depending on feeling... perception... preparations... consciousness."

The "I am" is not an innate self-knowledge. It is a construction, dependent on the aggregates. When the aggregates are seen as they are — as empty, as dependently arisen, as not-self — the "I am" has no place to stand.

There is a distinction between sakkāyadiṭṭhi (personality view) and asmimāna (the conceit "I am"). Personality view is the belief that there is a self — a fixed view. The conceit "I am" is subtler — it is the latent tendency to feel "I am" even without holding a view. The stream-winner has abandoned personality view but still has residual asmimāna. Only the Arahant has uprooted both.

---

### The Middle Path as Yathābhūta ñāṇadassana

The Buddha's middle path is not a compromise between extremes. It is a direct seeing of things as they are — yathābhūta. When you see the arising of the world as it is, the view of non-existence does not occur. When you see the cessation of the world as it is, the view of existence does not occur.

This seeing is not a view. It is a knowledge that does not depend on another (aparappaccayā ñāṇa). It is the knowledge that arises from direct experience of the dependent arising and ceasing of phenomena, without adding a self to them.

## Plain-English Notes

### The Gem in the Pond

You see a gem reflected in a pond. You think it's in the pond. You empty the pond, dig up the mud — no gem. The gem was never in the pond. It was a reflection.

Name-and-form is like that. It appears to be a self — a real "I" inside experience. But when you look for it, you find only the reflection. The "self" was never there. It was a reflection of consciousness on the aggregates.

### Asmīti — The "I Am" Thought

The thought "I am" is not a direct knowledge of a self. It's a dependent construction. It arises when form is grasped, when feeling is grasped, when perception, preparations, or consciousness is grasped. Without grasping, "I am" does not arise.

This is the practical meaning of anattā: not "there is no self" (a view), but "the 'I am' arises only when there is grasping" (a description of how experience works).

### Yathābhūta — Things As They Are

Not a philosophical position. Not "everything exists" or "nothing exists." Just: seeing arising as arising, ceasing as ceasing. This seeing, which is not dependent on any view, is the middle path.

## Meditations

### The Pond Meditation

Consider a belief you hold about yourself — "I am X." Now ask: is this a real gem in the pond, or a reflection?

The pond is the five aggregates. The reflection is the "I am." You empty the pond (investigate the aggregates) and find no gem (no self). The reflection was never a thing — it was a play of light on water.

Practice: when the thought "I am" arises, don't fight it. Just see: "This 'I am' depends on form, depends on feeling..." See which aggregate it's leaning on. When the leaning is seen, the "I am" has no support.
