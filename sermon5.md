# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 05

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*

---

## The Teaching

### The Razor-Sharpening Simile: Grasping vs. Light Handling

For further explanation of the technique of using concepts without dogmatic involvement, we may take up the two terms parāmasana and sammasana. It might be better to distinguish the meanings of these two terms with the help of a simile. Let us take up the razor, which is such a useful requisite in our meditative life.

There is a certain special way in sharpening a razor. With the idea of sharpening the razor, if one grabs it tightly and rubs it on the sharpening stone, it will only become blunt. Parāmasana — grasping, grabbing — is something like that.

What then is the alternative? A more refined and softer approach is required as meant by the term sammasana. There is a proper mode of doing it. One has to hold the razor in a relaxed way, as if one is going to throw it away. One holds it lightly, ready to let go of it at any time. But, of course, with mindfulness. The wrist, also, is not rigid, but relaxed. Hand is supple at the joints and easy to swing. Then with that readiness, one sharpens the razor, sliding it smoothly on the stone. First: up, up, up, then: down, down, down, and then: up down, up down, up down. The third combined movement ensures that those parts of the blade still untouched by the stone will also get duly sharpened.

It is in the same manner that the razor of insight wisdom has to be whetted on the sharpening stone of the Satipaṭṭhānasutta. Inward, inward, inward — outward, outward, outward — inward outward, inward outward. Or else: arising, arising, arising — ceasing, ceasing, ceasing — arising ceasing, arising ceasing.

This is an illustration for the method of reflection, or sammasana, introduced by the Buddha in the Satipaṭṭhānasutta. Words and concepts have to be made use of, for attaining Nibbāna. But here the aim is only the up-building of mindfulness and knowledge. Once their purpose is served, they can be dismantled without being a bother to the mind. This is the significance of the concluding phrase "He abides independent and does not cling to anything in the world."

### The Arahant: Neither Grasps Nor Gives Up

The Aṭṭhakavagga and the Pārāyanavagga of the Sutta Nipāta contain verses which are extremely deep. In the Aṭṭhakavagga, one often comes across apparently contradictory pairs of terms, side by side. About the Arahant it is said that: "he neither grasps nor gives up" — *nādeti na nirassati*. "There is nothing taken up or rejected by him" — *attaṃ nirattaṃ na hi tassa atthi*.

By the way, the word attaṃ in this context is derived from ādātta (ā + dā), by syncopation. It should not be mistaken as a reference to attā, or soul. Similarly, niratta is from as, to throw, nirasta, conveying the idea of giving up or putting down. There is nothing taken up or given up by the Arahant.

This is the significance of the middle path revealed by the Buddha. The Arahant is said to have neither a farther shore nor a hither shore:

> *Yassa pāraṃ apāraṃ vā, pārāpāraṃ na vijjati, vītaddaraṃ visaṃyuttaṃ, tam ahaṃ brūmi brāhmaṇaṃ.*
>
> "For whom there is neither a farther shore, nor a hither shore, nor both, who is undistressed and unfettered, him I call a Brahmin."

Our usual concept of an Arahant is of one who has crossed over the ocean of saṃsāra and is standing on the other shore. But here is something enigmatic. The Arahant has neither a farther shore, nor a hither shore, nor both.

This same theme recurs throughout the Uragasutta of the Sutta Nipāta, with its seventeen verses sharing the same refrain:

> *So bhikkhu jahāti orapāraṃ, urago jiṇṇamiva tacaṃ purāṇaṃ.*
>
> "That monk gives up the hither and the farther shore, as a snake sheds its worn-out skin."

The monk gives up both — not just the near shore (saṃsāra) but also the far shore (Nibbāna as a destination). He sheds both like a snake's skin.

### Āsava: The Influxes of Fermentation

Yet another important term that should receive attention in any discussion on Nibbāna is āsava. This is because the Arahant is often called a khīṇāsava, one whose āsavas are extinct. Āsavakkhayo, extinction of āsavas, is an epithet of Nibbāna. So the distinct feature of an Arahant is his extinction of āsavas.

Now, what does āsava mean? In ordinary life, this word is used to denote fermentation or liquor that has got fermented for a long time. If there is even a dreg of ferment in a vessel, it is enough to cause fermentation for any suitable raw material put into it. So also are the āsavas. They are like the residual dregs of the ebullient mass of defilements in beings, which have undergone fermentation for a long, long time in saṃsāra.

Very often, the word āsava is rendered by 'cankers' or 'influxes'. They are like the fermented yeast, which is capable of causing intoxication. In a being overwhelmed by influxes, there arises a deep delusion regarding the true nature of things. They have an intoxicating effect. The world is like a person drunk with the liquor of āsavas.

The cessation of existence — *bhavanirodho nibbānaṃ* — comes about by this extinction of influxes. The very existence of 'existence' is especially due to the flowing in of influxes of existence. What is called 'existence' is not the apparent process of existing visible to others. It is something that pertains to one's own mental continuum.

### Attention: The Arising and Ceasing of Mind-Objects

There is a discourse well worth remembering, because here the Buddha is dealing with the arising and cessation of the four objects used for establishing mindfulness. The Buddha declares:

> *Phassasamudayā vedanānaṃ samudayo, phassanirodhā vedanānaṃ atthagamo. Bhūtasamudayā rūpānaṃ samudayo, bhūtanirodhā rūpānaṃ atthagamo. Manasikārasamudayā dhammānaṃ samudayo, manasikāranirodhā dhammānaṃ atthagamo.*
>
> "With the arising of contact is the arising of feelings, with the cessation of contact is the going down of feelings. With the arising of the four great primaries is the arising of forms, with the cessation of the four great primaries is the going down of forms. With the arising of attention is the arising of mind-objects, and with the ceasing of attention is the going down of mind-objects."

The fact that mind-objects arise due to attention is noteworthy. All the mind-objects mentioned in the fourth section of contemplation arise when there is attention. And they go down when attention is not there. In other words, attending makes objects out of them. This way, we are reminded that, apart from making use of these words and concepts for the purpose of attaining Nibbāna, there is nothing worth holding on to or clinging to dogmatically.

### The Mind Is More Picturesque Than Any Picture

In the Gaddūlasutta and its parallel texts, the Buddha makes the following impressive declaration:

> *'Diṭṭhaṃ vo, bhikkhave, caraṇaṃ nāma cittan'ti? ... 'Tampi kho, bhikkhave, caraṇaṃ nāma cittaṃ citteneva cintitaṃ. Tenapi kho, bhikkhave, caraṇena cittena cittaññeva cittataraṃ. Tasmātiha, bhikkhave, abhikkhaṇaṃ sakaṃ cittaṃ paccavekkhitabbaṃ: Dīgharattam idaṃ cittaṃ saṃkiliṭṭhaṃ rāgena dosena mohenā'ti. Cittasaṃkilesā, bhikkhave, sattā saṃkilissanti, cittavodānā sattā visujjhanti.*
>
> "Monks, have you seen a picture called a movie (caraṇa)? ... Monks, even that picture called a movie is something thought out by the mind. But this mind, monks, is more picturesque than that picture called a movie. Therefore, monks, you should reflect moment to moment on your own mind with the thought: For a long time has this mind been defiled by lust, hate, and delusion. By the defilement of the mind, monks, are beings defiled. By the purification of the mind, are beings purified."

The mind is more marvellous than the most marvellous painting. Even the most beautiful painting or film is something thought out by the mind. But the mind itself — its capacity to create worlds — is even more marvellous. And yet, the same mind has been defiled by lust, hate, and delusion for a long time. The path is the purification of this mind.

### The Film Show: The Intoxication of Influxes

There is a strange thing about films and dramas. One goes to the cinema or the theatre saying: "I am going to see a film show, I am going to see a drama." And one returns saying: "I have seen a film show, I have seen a drama." But while the film show or the drama is going on, one forgets that one is seeing a show or a drama. Such a strange spell of delusion takes over. This is due to the intoxicating influence of influxes. If one wishes to enjoy a film show or a drama, one should be prepared to get intoxicated by it. Otherwise it will cease to be a film show or a drama for him.

What do the film producers and dramatists do? They prepare the background for eliciting the influxes of ignorance, latent in the minds of the audience. That is why such shows and performances are so powerful.

If the light of wisdom dawns on one's mind while watching a film show or a drama, one would even wonder whether it is actually a film or a drama, while others are enjoying it.

Speaking about the film show, we mentioned above that the spectator has entered into a world of his own creation. If we are to analyse this situation according to the law of dependent origination, we may add that in fact he has a consciousness and a name-and-form in line with the events of the story, based on the preparations in the midst of the darkness of ignorance. With all his experiences in seeing the film show, he is building up his five aggregates.

Therefore, when the light of wisdom comes and dispels the darkness of ignorance, a similar event can occur. One will come out of that plane of delusion.

The Buddha said: "Monks, this mind is luminous, but it is defiled by adventitious defilements." The mind is more marvellous than the most marvellous painting. Even a beautiful painting is something thought out by the mind. But the mind itself — its luminosity — is even more marvellous.

### The Six Qualities of the Dhamma

Now let us understand the significance of the six qualities of the Dhamma in the light of the Satipaṭṭhānasutta. The Dhamma is well-proclaimed, svākkhāta. It can be seen here and now, sandiṭṭhika. It is timeless, akālika. It invites one to come and see, ehipassika. It leads one onwards, opanayika. It can be understood by the wise each one by himself, paccattaṃ veditabbo viññūhi.

The method of the Satipaṭṭhāna is what makes these qualities real. The meditator contemplates arising and ceasing here and now, without needing to reference past lives. The razor of wisdom is sharpened on the stone of direct observation. The concepts used are scaffolding — once the building is up, the scaffolding is dismantled.

## Plain-English Notes

### Parāmasana vs. Sammasana (Grasping vs. Light Handling)

This is a teaching on *how* to use the Dhamma. If you grab the teachings tightly — "This is the truth, I must hold on to this" — you blunt the wisdom rather than sharpening it. The right approach is to hold lightly, ready to let go at any time, with a relaxed wrist and supple attention.

The three movements of the razor:
1. **Up, up, up** — attending inward (ajjhatta)
2. **Down, down, down** — attending outward (bahiddhā)
3. **Up down, up down** — attending both together (ajjhattabahiddhā)

This is the method of sammasana — a refined, repeated reflection that sharpens insight without grasping.

### The Arahant Has Neither Shore

The common image: the Arahant has crossed to the far shore of Nibbāna. The deeper teaching: the Arahant has *neither shore*. Why? Because the far shore only exists in relation to the near shore. If you've given up everything on this side *and* the idea of reaching something on the other side, there are no shores at all. The snake sheds its skin — it doesn't keep the old skin *or* hold on to a new one. It's just skinless.

This is the meaning of "neither grasps nor gives up." Grasping and giving up are a pair. The Arahant doesn't do either, because both depend on the same subject-object framework. When the framework is gone, neither action is possible.

### Āsava (Influxes/Fermentation)

The root metaphor is fermentation. A single yeast cell can ferment an entire vat. Similarly, the residual latency (anusaya) of craving, views, and ignorance can ferment the entire mass of experience into saṃsāra. The Arahant is "khīṇāsava" — the ferment is exhausted. Not suppressed, not managed — exhausted. There's nothing left to ferment.

The film show analogy: you go to the movies knowing it's a show, but during the show you forget. You build a world of name-and-form in your consciousness, keyed to the story. The influxes ferment the raw sensory data into a compelling reality. The Arahant sees the show as show, even while it's playing.

## Meditations

### 1. The Razor Meditation (Holding Lightly)

Sit with a single object of attention — the breath, a sensation, a thought. Now notice *how* you're attending.

**The wrong way:** Grab the object tightly. "I must be mindful! I must not miss a single breath!" Notice how this creates tension in the mind. The razor gets blunt.

**The right way:** Hold the object lightly. As if you're about to throw it away. The wrist is relaxed. You're ready to let go at any moment. The attention is present but not gripping.

Now practice the three movements:
- For a few minutes, attend **inwardly** — sensations inside the body, internal feelings, internal mind states.
- Then attend **outwardly** — sounds, the room, external sensations.
- Then attend **both together** — notice how internal and external are simultaneous, interpenetrating. They're not two separate spheres but one field.

**The logic this reveals:** The meditation object is not something to grab. It's a whetstone. You're not trying to possess the breath or own the sensation. You're using it to sharpen wisdom. When the wisdom is sharp, you let go of the stone.

---

### 2. The Film Show Meditation (Seeing Through the Intoxication)

Sit and watch your experience as if it's a film. The sights, sounds, thoughts, feelings — they're playing on the screen of awareness.

Now notice: during the film, you forget it's a film. You get angry at the villain, sad for the heroine, tense during the chase scene. You're *intoxicated* by the influxes. The film has produced a world for you.

Practice: while experiencing any strong emotion or compelling thought, remember: "This is a show." Don't suppress it. Don't try to end it. Just add the knowledge: "I know this is a show." Notice how the intoxicating quality diminishes when the light of wisdom is present.

**The logic this reveals:** The Buddha said the mind is luminous but defiled by adventitious defilements. The luminosity is always there — like the screen that doesn't change when the film changes. The defilements are like the movie — they appear on the screen but don't affect it. When you see the screen itself, the movie loses its power to intoxicate.

---

### 3. The Snake Shedding Meditation (Neither Shore)

Sit and contemplate a pair you're attached to: self/other, good/bad, inside/outside, past/future, saṃsāra/Nibbāna.

Now imagine you're a snake. You have an old skin — the near shore. It's worn out. You need to shed it.

But the snake doesn't just shed the old skin and stand there naked waiting for a new one. The snake sheds the skin and *moves on*. The "moving on" is not a new skin — it's the absence of the need for a skin.

Practice: Let go of one side of the pair. Notice: the other side immediately becomes less meaningful. Good only matters in relation to bad. Self only matters in relation to other. As you let go of both, you're not standing on either shore. You're not even in the water. There's just no shore-reference at all.

**The logic this reveals:** The Arahant has "neither a farther shore, nor a hither shore, nor both." The path doesn't take you from one place to another — it takes you out of the place-reference entirely. To "go beyond" is not to arrive somewhere else but to stop locating yourself.

---

### 4. The Fermentation Meditation (Exhausting the Yeast)

The āsavas are like yeast. Even a trace can ferment the whole. The Arahant has exhausted the yeast.

Practice: Sit and scan your mind for any residual "ferment" — a subtle liking, a subtle disliking, a subtle sense of "I am." Don't try to remove it. Just see it.

Now notice: this trace of ferment is enough to keep the whole process going. Even without active thoughts, this residual leaning is sufficient for consciousness to be "established." It's the second instance from the Cetanāsutta (sermon 3) — no intention, no construction, but latency remains.

The question: Can you see the latency without feeding it? The yeast needs sugar to ferment. If you don't add sugar (craving), the yeast just sits there. It doesn't disappear immediately, but it doesn't ferment anything either. This is the path — not killing the yeast, but starving it.

**The logic this reveals:** The Arahant doesn't destroy the āsavas by fighting them. The ferment exhausts itself when it's no longer fed. The exhaustion of influxes is not an annihilation — it's the natural end of a process that has run out of fuel.

---

## Summary of the Logic

Sermon 5 shifts from the *structure* of saṃsāra to the *method of practice*:

1. **How to use concepts:** Not by grasping (parāmasana) but by light handling (sammasana). The razor is sharpened by a relaxed wrist, not a tight grip. The three movements (inward, outward, both) refine the attention without dogmatic involvement.

2. **The Arahant's position:** No shore at all. Neither grasping nor giving up. The snake sheds its skin — it doesn't reach a new skin. This is the middle path that transcends the pair.

3. **Āsava as fermentation:** The influxes are like yeast — a trace ferments the whole. Existence itself is the bubbling of this fermentation. The end of āsavas is the end of existence, not as annihilation but as the exhaustion of the ferment.

4. **The film show:** Experience intoxicates us when we forget it's a show. The light of wisdom dispels the darkness of ignorance, and we see the show as show. The luminous mind was always there — the defilements were adventitious.
