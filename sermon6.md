# Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled: Sermon 06

**By Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda**

---

> *Etaṃ santaṃ, etaṃ paṇītaṃ, yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhakkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbānaṃ.*

---

## The Teaching

### The Five Similes of the Grasping Groups

In the Pheṇapiṇḍūpamasutta of the Khandhasaṃyutta, the summary verse says:

> *Pheṇapiṇḍūpamaṃ rūpaṃ, vedanā bubbuḷūpamā, marīcikūpamā saññā, saṅkhārā kadalūpamā, māyūpamañca viññāṇaṃ, dīpitādiccabandhunā.*
>
> "Form is like a mass of foam, feeling like a water bubble, perception like a mirage, preparations like a banana trunk, and consciousness like a magic show — so taught the Kinsman of the Sun."

These five similes bring out the insubstantiality of the five grasping groups. Their simulating and deceptive nature is indicated by the similes. Not only the magic show, but even the other similes, like the mass of foam, are suggestive of simulation, in giving a false notion of compactness. They all convey the idea of insubstantiality and deceptiveness. Consciousness in particular, is described in that context as a conjurer's trick.

In the Second Gaddūlasutta of the Saṃyutta Nikāya, the Buddha gives a simile of a painter:

> "Just as a dyer or a painter would fashion the likeness of a woman or of a man, complete in all its major and minor parts, on a well planed board, or a wall, or on a strip of cloth, with dye or lac or turmeric or indigo or madder, even so the untaught worldling creates, as it were, his own form, feelings, perceptions, preparations, and consciousness."

What the Buddha wants to convey by this comparison of the five grasping groups to an artefact done by a painter, is their insubstantiality and vanity. It brings out their compound and made-up nature.

---

### Saṅkhārā: "They Prepare the Prepared"

We come across a discourse in the Saṃyutta Nikāya, in which this primary sense of preparation in the word saṅkhāra is explicitly stated, namely the Khajjanīyasutta:

> *Kiñca, bhikkhave, saṅkhāre vadetha? Saṅkhatam abhisaṅkharontī'ti kho, bhikkhave, tasmā 'saṅkhārā'ti vuccanti.*
>
> "And what, monks, would you say are 'preparations'? They prepare the prepared — that, monks, is why they are called preparations. And what is the prepared that they prepare? They prepare form into the state of form, they prepare feeling into the state of feeling, they prepare perception into the state of perception, they prepare preparations into the state of preparations, they prepare consciousness into the state of consciousness."

This explains why saṅkhāras are so called. They are instrumental in building up each of these grasping groups. The most intriguing statement is that even the saṅkhāras themselves are built up by saṅkhāras — they prepare the prepared.

The two terms *abhisaṅkhataṃ* (specially prepared) and *abhisañcetayitaṃ* (thought out or intended) are often found in juxtaposition. Here we see the relationship of saṅkhāras to intention. The preparation is done by means of intentions. In the last analysis, it is something constructed by imagination. As suggested by the similes of the picture and the painter, these five groups turn out to be the products of imagination.

What supports this heaping up of preparations is ignorance. Ignorance provides the background, as in the case of the drama and the movie. The world attributes an activity to something by regarding it as a unit — by perceiving it as a compact unit. In other words, it is the way of the world to superimpose the concept of a unit or self-agency wherever there appears to be some sort of activity. As we mentioned in connection with the simile of the whirlpool, viewed from a distance, the whirlpool appears as a centre or a base. In the same way, wherever there appears to be some form of activity, we tend to bring in the concept of a unit. It is this very ignorance, this 'ignoring', that becomes the seed-bed for preparations. The basic presumption of this ignorance is that preparations must originate from a unitary centre.

---

### The Three Preparations: Breathing, Thinking, Feeling

The second link in the formula of dependent arising is defined by the Buddha not in terms of kamma but as three kinds of "preparation":

- **Kāyasaṅkhāro** (body-preparation): In-breaths and out-breaths. As the Arahant nun Dhammadinnā explains: "In-breaths and out-breaths are bodily, these things are bound up with the body, that is why in-breaths and out-breaths are a body-preparation." There is no explicit mention of kamma here.

- **Vacīsaṅkhāro** (speech-preparation): Thinking and pondering. "First having thought and pondered one breaks into speech, that is why thinking and pondering are a speech-preparation."

- **Cittasaṅkhāro** (mind-preparation): Perception and feeling. "Perception and feeling are mental, they are bound up with the mind, that is why perception and feeling are a mind-preparation."

The sense of 'preparation' is therefore quite apt. Before breaking out into speech, one thinks and ponders — this prepares the speech. Breathing is the most basic bodily preparation — in all our bodily activities, particularly when exerting ourselves, we sometimes take a deep breath, almost impulsively. Perception and feeling give the impetus for the arising of the more prominent stage of intention.

Beneath our heap of body actions, verbal actions, and mental acts of willing or intentions lies a huge mountain of activities. Breathing in and breathing out is the most basic activity in one's life. It is, in fact, the criterion for judging whether one is alive or dead.

### The Cetanāsutta: Ignorance as the Background

In the Cetanāsutta of the Sañcetaniyavagga of the Aṅguttara Nikāya, the Buddha analyses how ignorance underlies all preparations.

**First section:** "Monks, when the body is there, due to bodily intention, there arises inward pleasure and pain. When speech is there, due to verbal intention, there arises inward pleasure and pain. When mind is there, due to mental intention, there arises inward pleasure and pain — all conditioned by ignorance."

Given the concept of a body, due to intentions based on that concept, one experiences pleasure and pain. What is called 'the body' is a huge mass of activity, something like a big workshop or a factory. But because of ignorance, if one takes it as one thing — a unit — then there is room for bodily intention to come in. One can objectify the body and arouse thoughts of the body. Thereby one experiences pleasure and pain.

Similarly with speech and mind. Speech is a conglomeration of letters and words, but when taken as a real unit, one can form intentions about speech and experience pleasure and pain. The mind is not an entity by itself, like a soul. It is again only a heap of thoughts. But if one grants that there is a mind, due to that very presumption, one experiences inwardly pleasure and pain with mind as its object.

**Second section:** Either one oneself prepares that preparation, or others prepare it for him. Either one is fully aware, or fully unaware. In all cases, the preparation occurs.

**Third section (the most significant):** "Monks, in all these cases, ignorance hangs on. But with the remainderless fading away and cessation of ignorance, that body is not there, owing to which there can arise for him inward pleasure or pain, that speech is not there, that mind is not there. That field is not there, that site is not there, that base is not there, that reason is not there, owing to which there can arise for him inward pleasure or pain."

Since all the instances mentioned earlier are accompanied by ignorance, the utter fading away and cessation of that very ignorance removes the field, the ground, the base and the provenance for the arising of inward pleasure and pain.

With the cessation of ignorance comes the cessation of preparations. Thereby all pleasures and pains cease. This, in other words, is the state of Nibbāna.

---

### The Drama and the Background of Ignorance

Now what supports this deceptive and delusive quality of preparations is ignorance. All this 'acting' that is going on in the world is kept up by ignorance, which provides the background for it. Just as, in a drama, such preparations as change of dress, make-up contrivances, character portrayal, and stage-craft, create an atmosphere of delusion, so also are the saṅkhāras instrumental in building up these five grasping groups.

To hark back to two lines of a verse: *mohasambandhano loko, bhabbarūpo va dissati* — "the world appears as real to one who is fettered to delusion." This means that the world has an apparent reality, that it merely gives the impression of something real to one who is deluded. Realism, in the context of art and drama, amounts to an apparent reality. It connotes the skill in deceiving the audience. It is, in fact, only a show of reality. The successful drama is one that effectively hoodwinks an audience.

Now if at any point of time that ignorance happens to cease, then there will be no pleasure or displeasure for the audience, however much make-up and pretension there is. It is such a situation of non-enjoyment that occurred when Upatissa (who later became Venerable Sāriputta) and Kolita (who later became Venerable Moggallāna) witnessed a hill-top festival. While others were enjoying the festivities, they alone were not captivated. They had a flash of insight due to the light of wisdom that came from within, not due to any illumination from outside. Because of it, those preparations ceased to be preparations for them. This is how the Dhamma-eye arises — by seeing through the preparations, not by adding more.

From this we can understand that the term saṅkhāra becomes meaningful only against the background of ignorance.

## Plain-English Notes

### The Five Similes

| Aggregate | Simile | Meaning |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Form (rūpa) | Foam | Looks solid from a distance, but is just bubbles packed together |
| Feeling (vedanā) | Bubble | Arises and pops instantly |
| Perception (saññā) | Mirage | Appears to be something it's not — water on the road |
| Preparations (saṅkhārā) | Banana trunk | Layer after layer, no core |
| Consciousness (viññāṇa) | Magic show | A conjurer's trick — seems to be one thing but is something else |

Each simile exposes the *insubstantiality* and *deceptiveness* of what we take to be solid, real, and personal.

### Saṅkhārā = "They Prepare the Prepared"

The definition is circular but profound: preparations prepare the prepared. They make form into "form," feeling into "feeling," etc. They're the activity of constructing what then appears as a thing.

The iceberg analogy: most of saṅkhārā is below the surface. Above: conscious intentions, actions, speech. Below: breathing, thinking, perceiving, feeling — the subliminal preparatory activity that keeps the whole show running.

### The Three Preparations Are Not Kamma

This is a crucial distinction. The second link of dependent arising (saṅkhārā) is defined in the suttas as:
- In-and-out breathing (body-preparation)
- Thinking and pondering (speech-preparation)
- Perception and feeling (mind-preparation)

Not "good deeds" or "bad deeds" — but the *most basic activities of being alive*. Breathing, thinking, feeling. These prepare the ground for everything else.

### Ignorance as the Background

The Cetanāsutta says: body, speech, and mind are the fields, but ignorance hangs on all of them. When ignorance ceases, the fields disappear — not because the body vanishes, but because the concept of "body-as-unit" is no longer projected onto the activity.

This is the key: the body is a mass of activity, not a thing. Ignorance compresses it into a "unit." When ignorance ceases, the compression stops. The activity continues but is no longer mistaken for a self or a thing.

## Meditations

### 1. The Five Similes Meditation

Take each aggregate and contemplate its simile for a few minutes:

**Form as foam:** Look at your hand. See it as millions of cells packed together, constantly dying and being replaced. From a distance, it looks solid — one hand. Up close, it's foam.

**Feeling as bubble:** Notice a feeling arise. Watch it pop. How long did it last? A few seconds? A minute? Like a bubble in rain — there, then gone.

**Perception as mirage:** Look at an object. The perception "this is a cup" — is it the cup itself, or your mind's interpretation of sensory data? Like a mirage, perception gives the impression of something that isn't actually there in the way it appears.

**Preparations as banana trunk:** Peel back one thought — what's underneath? Another thought. Peel that — another. Layer after layer, no core. The "doer" is just more preparations.

**Consciousness as magic show:** Watch awareness itself. It seems to be one thing — a constant witness. But it's a conjurer's trick, producing world after world.

**The logic this reveals:** None of the five has any substance. They appear solid but are simulacra. The "person" is a magic show performed by emptiness.

---

### 2. The Body-as-Workshop Meditation

The Cetanāsutta says the body is like a big workshop or factory. Sit and feel your body not as "your body" but as a *site of activity*:
- Breathing (the most basic — is the workshop running?)
- Heart beating
- Sensations arising and passing
- Muscles tensing and releasing

Now notice: when you call this "my body," you're compressing all this activity into a single unit — a unit called "me." That compression is ignorance. Without it, there's just activity: no owner, no worker, just the workshop running.

**Practice:** For 10 minutes, try to experience the body as pure activity. Don't say "my breath" — say "there is breathing." Don't say "my leg" — say "there is hardness, there is pressure." See if you can keep the workshop running without a foreman.

**The logic this reveals:** The "unit" of self is an imposition on an otherwise continuous field of activity. When the compression stops, there's no one inside the workshop. But the workshop keeps running — breathing, walking, eating, speaking. This is the Arahant's body: active, but ownerless.

---

### 3. The Iceberg Meditation (Subliminal Preparations)

Beneath conscious thought lies a mountain of subliminal activity: breathing, subtle thinking, perception, feeling. Like an iceberg, most of saṅkhārā is below the surface.

Sit and try to sense the subliminal level:
- Feel the breath happening *without you doing it*
- Notice thoughts arising *before* you "think" them
- Feel perception occurring — the raw recognizing of things — before you name them
- Notice feeling tones (pleasant/unpleasant/neutral) arising automatically

These are the preparations. They prepare the ground for everything else. They're happening whether you're aware of them or not.

**Practice:** Don't try to control them. Just witness the preparatory activity. Breathing prepares the body for action. Subtle thinking prepares speech. Subtle feeling prepares intention.

**The logic this reveals:** The "you" who seems to be the doer is actually the last stage of a long preparatory process. By the time a conscious intention arises, the preparations have already been preparing it. The Arahant sees through the entire iceberg — not just the tip.

---

### 4. The Extinguished Film Show Meditation

The Cetanāsutta's third section: when ignorance ceases completely, the field for pleasure and pain is removed. Not because the body, speech, and mind disappear, but because they're no longer grasped as "units."

Sit and watch a thought arise. Notice: there is the thought, and there is the *taking* of the thought as "mine" or "real." The second movement — the grasping — is ignorance in action.

Now try: let the thought arise, but don't let ignorance "hang on" to it. Don't compress it into a unit. Let it be just activity — appearing and disappearing without being claimed.

If you can do this with one thought, you've seen what happens when ignorance ceases for a moment. The show continues, but no one is intoxicated by it.

**The logic this reveals:** Nibbāna is not the end of experience — it's the end of the intoxication that ignorance produces. The body breathes, the mind thinks, feelings arise — but without the background of ignorance, none of this produces "inward pleasure and pain." The field is removed, not the crops.

---

## Summary of the Logic

Sermon 6 focuses on the nature of saṅkhārā (preparations):

1. **The five aggregates are simulacra** — foam, bubble, mirage, banana trunk, magic show. They appear to be what they are not. The painter simile shows them as artefacts, not realities.

2. **Saṅkhārā defined operationally:** "They prepare the prepared." They're not just "mental formations" — they're the activity of constructing experience itself. Breathing, thinking, perceiving, feeling — these are the preparations that build the world of the five aggregates.

3. **Ignorance is the background:** The Cetanāsutta shows that body, speech, and mind become "fields" for pleasure and pain *because ignorance hangs on them*. When ignorance ceases, the fields disappear. Not the body, but the body-as-unit.

4. **The three preparations are basic life activities** — not kamma. Breathing, thinking-and-pondering, and perception-and-feeling. The iceberg is mostly below the surface.

5. **The film show ceases to intoxicate** when the light of wisdom comes on. The preparations remain as preparations, but they no longer produce inward pleasure and pain because the background of ignorance is gone.
